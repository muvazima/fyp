@&#MAIN-TITLE@&#Robust simultaneous positive data clustering and unsupervised feature selection using generalized inverted Dirichlet mixture models

@&#HIGHLIGHTS@&#


               
               
                  
                     
                        
                           
                           An algorithm for simultaneous clustering, feature selection and outliers is proposed.


                        
                        
                           
                           The proposed approach is based on finite generalized inverted Dirichlet mixture.


                        
                        
                           
                           An approach for model selection using minimum message length is developed.


                        
                        
                           
                           The model is applied to the challenging problems of visual scenes and objects clustering.

@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  The discovery, extraction and analysis of knowledge from data rely generally upon the use of unsupervised learning methods, in particular clustering approaches. Much recent research in clustering and data engineering has focused on the consideration of finite mixture models which allow to reason in the face of uncertainty and to learn by example. The adoption of these models becomes a challenging task in the presence of outliers and in the case of high-dimensional data which necessitates the deployment of feature selection techniques. In this paper we tackle simultaneously the problems of cluster validation (i.e. model selection), feature selection and outliers rejection when clustering positive data. The proposed statistical framework is based on the generalized inverted Dirichlet distribution that offers a more practical and flexible alternative to the inverted Dirichlet which has a very restrictive covariance structure. The learning of the parameters of the resulting model is based on the minimization of a message length objective incorporating prior knowledge. We use synthetic data and real data generated from challenging applications, namely visual scenes and objects clustering, to demonstrate the feasibility and advantages of the proposed method.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

@&#BACKGROUND@&#

Clustering is a powerful technique for knowledge discovery and data mining and several theoretical (see, for instance, [1–3]) and experimental studies [4] have been conducted in the past. The main goal is to identify patterns and features reflecting the regularities in data via unsupervised classification of data items into homogenous groups called clusters such that items in the same cluster are similar to each other and items in different clusters tend to be dissimilar (i.e. the intra-group similarities are high and the inter-group similarities are low). Many clustering approaches are available and can be roughly grouped into two categories. The first category contains heuristic algorithms where no probabilistic models are explicitly assumed (e.g. K-Means). The second category contains model-based methods which make inference via probabilistic assumptions of the data distributions. Finite mixture is the most popular model-based approach to clustering and offers great practical value in modeling heterogenous data [5,6]. It has received an increasing interest in diverse fields such as artificial intelligence, pattern recognition, computer vision, data mining, information retrieval, biology, and statistics. The use of finite mixture models provides a formal approach to unsupervised learning and allows the incorporation of prior knowledge into cluster analysis which results in more meaningful clusters. Indeed, the consideration of mixture models allows to express cluster membership probabilistically; that is, a vector of features representing a given object to classify is not assigned to a unique cluster; instead, it has a probability to be affected to each of the possible clusters where the sum of all cluster membership probabilities of each vector must sum to one.

One of the challenging and important aspects of knowledge discovery in general and cluster analysis in particular is the weighting and selection of variables [7–9]. Feature selection (i.e. the process of selecting the most informative features) has been shown to be a crucial step in several applications such as the analysis of gene expression data [10–12], fraud detection [13], text categorization [14,5,15], and user profiling [16]. This is especially true for applications generating high-dimensional data which modeling has been the subject of extensive research efforts in the past [17–24]. Indeed, it is well-known that irrelevant variables hurt generally clustering models performance and cause significant over-fitting [25–27]. Feature selection is a large research area and different approaches have been proposed in both supervised and unsupervised settings. These approaches can be roughly classified into two categories namely filters and wrappers [6]. While filter methods select feature subsets independently from the learning algorithm, wrappers perform selection by evaluating candidate features subsets according to the performance of the learning algorithm. Of particular interest for us will be simultaneous clustering and feature selection using finite mixture models which has received some attention recently (see, for instance, [28,6] and references therein). The main motivation of these recent works was the fact that the feature selection problem is very related to the model selection task and then have to be performed simultaneously [29–31].

The estimation of the number of components (i.e. model selection or cluster validity) in the case of finite mixture models has been studied theoretically and experimentally in different ways (see, for instance, [32]). The most popular approaches have been based on the consideration of penalized likelihood methods where a penalizing term is added to the likelihood such as minimum description length (MDL) [33] and minimum message length (MML) [34] criteria. In particular, MML-based learning has been the subject of extensive research recently and used for simultaneous clustering and feature selection in [28,6] within statistical frameworks adopting finite Gaussian mixture and finite generalized Dirichlet mixture models, respectively. While the approach in [28] has been devoted mainly to Gaussian data and renewed interest in unsupervised feature selection using finite mixture models, the work in [6] has concerned proportional data modeling. Recent works, however, have shown that other kinds of data are of particular importance. This is especially true for positive data which are naturally generated by several real life applications as thoroughly discussed in [35], for instance, where a statistical model based on finite inverted Dirichlet mixture has been proposed for the clustering and modeling of such data. The inverted Dirichlet offers high flexibility and ease of use for the modeling of positive data [35–38]. It suffers, however, from a major drawback namely its very restrictive strictly positive covariance structure as we shall see in the next section where we propose the consideration of the generalized inverted Dirichlet (GID) to overcome this limitation, which can be viewed as the first contribution of our work.

The GID has several interesting mathematical properties which allow, for instance, the representation of GID samples in a transformed space in which features are independent and follow inverted Beta distributions. Indeed, as a second contribution, we demonstrate that the conditional independence assumption among features commonly used by researchers, by assuming a diagonal covariance matrix in the case of the Gaussian for instance [28], in modeling high-dimensional data becomes a fact for GID data sets without loss of accuracy. This interesting advantage of the GID is used in this paper to develop a statistical model for simultaneous high-dimensional positive data clustering and feature selection which is the third main contribution of our paper. Unlike previous simultaneous clustering and feature selection approaches, another principal focus of the developed model is outliers (i.e. data that do not belong to any of the clusters) detection (i.e. ability to deal with noisy data) which is a crucial problem in many real knowledge representation-based applications. Indeed, it is well-known that clustering performance can be very sensitive to outliers [39–42] and that resulting clustering models and selected features may be inaccurate in noisy environments [43–45]. Other contributions of our work are the development of a principled learning framework to estimate the parameters of the resulting model, and its application to two challenging problems namely visual scenes and objects clustering.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, our simultaneous clustering, feature selection and outliers detection model is proposed and discussed in details. In Section 3, we develop a MML-based approach to learn the proposed model by choosing its appropriate priors and computing the related Fisher information. Extensive simulations and experiments are conducted in Section 4 to show the merits of the proposed work. Discussions and conclusion follow in Section 5.

In this section, we start by presenting the finite GID mixture model. Next, we show how feature selection and outliers rejection can be integrated into it.

In this section, we shall focus on two challenging problems related to multimedia data organization namely visual scenes and visual objects clustering in order to evaluate the merits of our work.

The visual scenes clustering application is based on the data set originally collected in [66] from which five categories were used in our experiments namely living room, coasts, forest, mountain, and tall building (see Fig. 4

The main goal of this application is to investigate the effects of outliers on the performance of the clustering algorithm for positive real data sets using GID mixture model. Also, the performance of GID mixture has been compared with GMM model.

An important part of visual scenes clustering problem is feature extraction. Many global and local visual descriptors have been proposed in the past. Here, we use local Histogram of Oriented Gradient (HOG) descriptor [67] which generates positive features and which we found efficient and convenient for our applications. Experiments are conducted by considering three windows for the HOG descriptor which allows to represent each image by 81-dimensional vector of features. We conduct 5-fold cross-validation experiments, then we report the number of components returned by the algorithm, features relevancies, and the confusion matrix for each experiment.

In the first experiment, we compare the performance of GID and GMM mixture models without taking into consideration the relevancy of features nor the presence of outliers. Table 8
                            shows the confusion matrix when GID model is used. According to this table, the number of misclassified images is 85 which gives us an accuracy of 71.19%. The run time in the GMM case was 3.39s. Both models were not able to detect the outlier images as a different class. In fact, all outliers have been affected to the five main classes (see Table 9

The second experiment is conducted by taking into consideration the relevancy of features but without performing outliers detection for both GID mixture and GMM. Fig. 6

Comparing the results of the first and the second experiments, we conclude that the feature selection process improves the classification results. However, the outliers have not been affected to a separate cluster.

In the third experiment, we consider both the relevancy of features and the presence of outliers. Fig. 7

@&#CONCLUSION@&#

Recent advances in processing and networking capabilities of computers have caused an accumulation of immense amounts on multimodal multimedia data (image, text, video). These data are generally presented as high-dimensional vectors of features. The availability of these high-dimensional data sets has provided the input to a large variety of statistical learning efforts including clustering, classification, feature selection, outliers detection and density estimation. In this context, a finite mixture offers a formal approach to clustering and a powerful tool to tackle the problem of data modeling. It is often applied in signal and image processing, as well as machine learning and data mining applications. Some special concerns when considering mixture models are the choice of the components densities, the determination of the number of components, the selection of the relevant features and the detection of potential outliers. Despite the fact that all these concerns are interrelated, they have been tackled separately in the past. The goal of this paper is to propose a framework in which all these problems are tackled simultaneously in the case of positive data modeling. The developed statistical framework is based on the finite GID mixture model and learned via the minimization of a message length objective by deploying an EM framework. The great virtue of our learning approach is that it allows to produce a compromise between goodness of fit and model’s complexity. Empirical results which involve generated data as well as real applications concerning visual scenes and objects classification show that the proposed approach is promising. We are currently working on the development of a purely nonparametric Bayesian inference alternative, like the one developed in [69], for the learning of our model. The main goal is to overcome problems related to local convergence, dependency on initialization, and model selection which may be caused by the greedy nature of our algorithm. The learning within this Bayesian inference framework will be based on Monte Carlo simulation methods which have increasingly been recognized by researchers as indispensable tools for several computational problems. Several other directions present themselves for future efforts. Indeed, future works could be devoted to the application of the proposed work to problems where the rare events (i.e. outliers) are more interesting than inliers such as detection of credit card fraud and monitoring of criminal activities (e.g. intrusion detection) [70], to the development of a hierarchical clustering approach like the one proposed in [71], to the integration of the GID mixture within a semi-supervised framework by combining learning from labeled and unlabeled data, or to the extension of the proposed work to online settings using for instance one of the techniques recently discussed in [72] which shall allow simultaneous dynamic features weighting and outliers detection.
