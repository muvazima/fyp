@&#MAIN-TITLE@&#A delayed product customization cost model with supplier delivery performance

@&#HIGHLIGHTS@&#


               
               
                  
                     
                        
                           
                           We model the costs and benefits of delayed product customization with supplier delivery performance.


                        
                        
                           
                           The optimal position of the customization point depends on the boundary of the supplier delivery window.


                        
                        
                           
                           The choice of the service level at the customization point affects the total operating cost.


                        
                        
                           
                           Keeping the boundary of the delivery window fixed will decrease the customization point.


                        
                        
                           
                           A non-constant service level impacts the customization point.


                        
                     
                  
               
            

@&#KEYPHRASES@&#

Delayed product differentiation

Supplier lead time delivery window

Product customization

@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  The concept of delayed product differentiation has received considerable attention in the research literature in recent years. However, few analytical models explain and quantify the benefits of delayed product differentiation strategy with additional consideration of supplier delivery performance. This paper proposes a delayed product differentiation model in which a supply of raw materials is integrated at the beginning of the production process to match uncertain demand in a cost-effective way given the constraint of lead time delivery window. It develops insights regarding a delayed product differentiation strategy and shows that with respect to delivery windows, supplier delivery performance plays an important role in the determination of the optimal point of differentiation. This study also shows that when the “on-time” and the “late” portions of the delivery window are constant, the proposed cost function coincides with similar models found in the literature. An extension of this work also reveals that when the customer service level varies across various production stages, its choice affects the decision to delay or postpone the customization point. A mini industrial case involving the customization of a personal desktop computer is used to illustrate the applicability of the resulting framework.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

With increasing customization needs, the ability to accurately forecast customer demand for different products, to provide high customer service while maintaining low inventories has emerged as an issue of concern for top management in today's global market (Chase, Aquilano, & Jacobs, 1998). Faced with expanding product variety due to increasing demand for customized products, most companies struggle to offer products of top quality, to manage costs and effectively compete. Product customization calls for an elaborate strategy to face some of these challenges and it can be realized by one of the practical methods, namely delayed product differentiation (also known as form postponement) (da Cunha, Agard, & Kusiak, 2007). According to Lee and Tang (1998), product differentiation is the split of products into separate items when components with different technical specifications are created, multiple end products with diverse functionalities are created, a product is packaged in different ways, or the exact same item is stored in different geographical locations. In this paper, we focus on the application of form postponement in product customization, or “delayed product customization,’’ which relies on delaying as much as possible the point where a work-in-process inventory is committed to a particular end-product.

Form postponement has grown in popularity among supply chain practitioners and presents a number of benefits during product customization. For example: it attempts to reduce the risks associated with product variety by exploiting the commonality between items and by designing the production and distribution processes to delay the point of differentiation (Anand & Girota, 2007; Granot & Yin, 2008; Lee, 1996; Ma, Wang, & Liming, 2002; Ngniatedema, 2010, 2012). It also provides the flexibility for holding the right inventory at the right place and in the right form, therefore enabling a company to minimize supply chain risks and to respond more quickly to market demand while lowering inventory costs (Aviv & Federgruen, 2001; Christopher, Christopher, & Hanna, 2007; Garg & Tang, 1997; Lee & Tang, 1997; Nair, 2005; Ngniatedema & Chakravarthy, 2013; Sánchez & Pérez, 2005; Shao & Ji, 2008; Yang, Burns, & Backhouse, 2004).


                     Lee and Tang (1997) proposed a delayed differentiation model for a production process in which two products can be manufactured in a series of N discrete stages and inventories can be stored in a buffer following each operation. The processes from the first to the kth stage are defined as the common production processes, whereas those from the (k + 1)th to end stages are called differentiated production processes for each individual product. The point k, also known as differentiation point, is assumed to be the point of last common operation after which the products are differentiated. An increase in the value of k implies that the postponement timing is deferred in the entire production process.

However, their model, useful for analyzing the costs and benefits of delayed product differentiation, has a common major weakness in that it fails to address the possible risks associated with the supply of raw materials at the beginning of the production process. Ignoring such factors during postponement implementation can lead to considerable sunk costs and hence should be treated cautiously.

In this article, we complement Lee and Tang's (1997) deficiency by incorporating the delivery of components from an external supplier at the beginning of the production network. This additional consideration is depicted in Fig. 1 with the point “0” designed to hold inventory of incoming components. We then introduce the following two random variables: (1) the quantity of raw materials received from the supplier prior to the start of the production process and, (2) the lead time delivery window, respectively. Our study also goes beyond Lee and Tang's (1997) decoupling assumption of using constant customer service levels at different production stages to examine the effect of non-constant service levels on postponement decisions.

Building from previous modeling approaches, (e.g. Guiffrida & Nagi, 2006), we use the following notations at the point “0” of the production network: c

It also defines the benchmark provided by the manufacturer and it is used as criterion to classify deliveries as being early, on-time, or late. The former specifies a delivery range through c

According to these authors, a delivery lead time consists of the internal manufacturing lead time(s) of the supplier plus the external lead time associated with transporting the delivery of raw materials from the supplier to manufacturer.

Under these considerations, we contribute to the literature by introducing two additional costs components which are likely to impact the total customization cost. The first cost results from the buffer inventory coming from the supplier side, whereas the second one results from the impact of supplier reliability with respect to the firm's specification of delivery timeliness. Technically speaking, we introduce two important random variables to the delayed differentiation framework during a postponement scenario: (i) the quantity of raw materials coming from the supply side to match customer demand and (ii) the supplier lead time that follows a general distribution, thus complementing the literature on supplier lead time delivery performance with respect to the on-time delivery window (Guiffrida & Nagi, 2006). We also examine a special case where the supplier lead time is normally distributed and important conclusions are derived. According to (Guiffrida & Nagi, 2006), “delivery performance” refers to delivery timeliness to the final customer in a serial supply chain that is operating under a centralized management structure. In the remainder of this paper, delivery performance refers to the delivery timeliness of raw materials to the point “0” of Fig. 1 introduced in Kadje (2012).

The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we present Lee and Tang's (1997) model in the context of product customization and we recall Guiffrida and Nagi's (2006) model on lead time delivery performance with respect to the delivery window. We then highlight some shortcomings in these two streams of research followed by our new modeling assumptions and the new average total cost function for delayed product differentiation. Section 3 contains the analytical study of the proposed cost function in product customization scenarios. In Section 4, the model is illustrated using an industry example involving the customization of a personal desktop computer, whereas Section 5 contains some concluding remarks. The proofs of important findings can be found in
Appendix A.

We evaluate the supplier delivery performance within our model using the supplier delivery windows. The literature uses penalty cost based models to evaluate the supplier delivery performance. These models analyze delivery timeliness to the final customer with regard to the customer's specification of an on-time delivery window by translating the probability of untimely delivery into expected cost measures. The modeling approaches differ in the type of distribution chosen for the supplier delivery window.

One stream of research assumes that the supplier delivery window follows a special distribution: the case of normally distributed delivery window can be found in Guiffrida and Nagi (2006), and the case of Gamma distribution can be found in Bushuev, Guiffrida, and Shanker (2011). On another modeling approach, Bushuev and Guiffrida (2012) examine the optimal positioning of delivery window when the distribution of the supplier delivery window has a closed form. We contribute to this literature by introducing a delivery window that follows a general random distribution.

Historically, conditional expectation approaches are used in the supplier delivery window literature for modeling supplier performance metrics. A careful analysis of these stream of research shows that the proposed cost-based performance metrics can be broken down in three cost components: (i) the holding costs due to early deliveries and which contribute to excess inventory, (ii) the production stoppages costs due to late deliveries, and (iii) a zero cost for delivery within the delivery window. A major weakness emerging from these studies is the lack of consideration of the likelihood of raw materials delivery outside or inside the agree upon delivery window. Therefore, the cost models presented in past studies do not account for important supply risks, approaches which can lead to possible inaccurate conclusions. When the delayed differentiation strategy is used in a product customization scenario, knowledge of the likelihood of supply risks is important for planning purposes. To this end, we contribute to the literature by proposing a simple, yet pragmatic framework based on Probability Theory and using the expected value concept to develop a cost-based performance metric related to a supplier delivery window.

Before concluding this work, we illustrate our study using a numerical example in the general case where the late and early delivery dates depend on k. This is due to the fact that the analysis of the total cost function in that case is a complex problem.

The literature documents a number of successful case studies on delayed product customization, especially in the computer industry. Hewlett Packard is a classic example who successfully used delayed differentiation for final printer configuration (Aviv & Federgruen, 2001; Feitzinger & Lee, 1997); Dell is also well known for their success because it changed the landscape of the PC industry using postponement. By adopting postponement as one of its core strategies, Dell was able to achieve the flexibility needed to develop different version of products based on individual customer tastes. This strategy helps Dell to minimize their inventory and to generate cash much faster than any other company in the PC industry (Chopra & Van Mieghem, 2000; Fugate & Mentzer, 2004; Radstaak & Ketelaar, 1998). Other cases are cited for a PC manufacturer in China (Huang & Lo, 2003). Rietze (2006) also presented several case studies on the application of postponement in product customization for companies such as Xilinx, General Motors, Honda, Polaroid, and Reebook, to name a few.

To illustrate our model, we limit this study to the application of delayed differentiation in desktop PC customization.

The literature suggests that a modern desktop PC uses at least twenty components (Kumar, 2004; Rietze, 2006; Wu, 2006). To understand the decision making process in a desktop PC assembly, we conducted interviews with computer professionals in a Midwestern city and at a computer help desk of a metropolitan university in the Midwest. Participants were presented with the list of components of a modern desktop PC collected from the literature. Based on their responses, the assembly of a modern desktop PC (i.e. PC for Y2K) starts with the critical internal components that affect decision making during the assembly process in the following sequence: (i) motherboard, (ii) CPU, (iii) memory, (iv) video card, to which other secondary components and peripherals can be added later, (v) case, and finally (vi) other peripherals and internal components such as the hard drive or the optical drive (Kumar, 2004; Rietze, 2006). In some cases, the video card and the expansion cards, as well as the power supply are integrated on the motherboard and should be skipped on the previous assembly description.                         Table 5
                         summarizes the results for this illustrative example involving the customization of a desktop PC. A separate cost Z(k) is also computed for comparison purposes because this study extends Lee and Tang's (1997) delayed differentiation cost model.

The results from this study show that:

(i) If z = 0.8, from the cost function Z(k) suggested in Lee and Tang (1997), the optimal point of differentiation (the point where a firm starts the customization of each individual product with the most pay-off) is 3 (k* = 3). However, when the supply of raw materials and a supplier delivery window are integrated into the delayed differentiation framework, our study demonstrates that the optimal point of differentiation is earlier in the production process at stage 2 (k* = 2).

(ii) If z = 1.65, the optimal point of differentiation from Lee and Tang (1997) using the cost function Z(k) is 5 (k* = 5) and the optimal point of differentiation using the cost function C(k) is 3 (k* = 3).

This new finding suggests that when the early and late delivery dates of the delivery window are not free of the differentiation point (i.e. they are not expressed by a function of k), we do not recommend Lee & Tang's model for the determination of the optimal differentiation point. Hence, integrating the supply of raw materials as well as the supplier on the delayed product differentiation cost model affects the position where the manufacturer should start customizing each individual product with the most pay-off.

The cost of using delayed differentiation strategy is important to the manufacturer during product customization. In addition to the customer demand, this cost interrelates with other factors such as the supplier lead time delivery window, the supply of raw materials and the inventory levels at the beginning of the manufacturing process.

In this study, we establish the conditions under which the cost of using the form postponement strategy in product customization can be improved when the supplier lead time delivery window, the supply of raw materials and the inventory levels at the beginning of the manufacturing process are integrated in the delayed differentiation framework. By integrating these factors in the existing models, we demonstrate that they are indeed relevant when finding the optimal point of differentiation from economic perspectives. We show that when the lower bound and the upper bound of the supplier delivery window depend on the differentiation point, results from existing models can be misleading when finding the point where a firm can customize individual products with the most pay-off. Our study also shows that the value of a postponement strategy grows with the choice of the customer service. Since it has been widely observed that firms use postponement to improve customer service and to effectively compete, we showed that the choice of the service level at each inventory buffer point is very important from a financial perspective when the managers decide to postpone or not postpone the differentiation.

The implications from this theoretical and analytical contribution help complement previous studies on the benefits of using delayed differentiation in product customization. This contribution (i) explains and evaluates the impact of supplier reliability on the cost of offering customized products (ii) establishes the conditions under which customization can be postponed or anticipated and (iii) determines the differentiation point where customization can be achieved with the most pay-offs.

Form postponement is of great theoretical and practical interest. It is our hope that this work will facilitate a greater understanding of this important strategy for product customization and other supply chain strategies and highlight potential opportunities for building a research agenda in this area.
