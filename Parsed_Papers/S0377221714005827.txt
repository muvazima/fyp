@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  We analyze the role of quality, which we define as an attribute of a product that increases consumers’ willingness to buy, as a competitive tool in a quality-price setting. We consider an incumbent’s entry-deterrence strategies using quality as a deterrent when faced by a potential entrant. We investigate settings motivating the incumbent to blockade the entrant (i.e., prevent entry without extra effort), deter the entrant (i.e., prevent entry with extra effort), or accommodate the entrant (i.e., allow the entry to take place). We identify conditions under which the incumbent may actually over-invest in quality to deter entrance. More interestingly, we also identify conditions under which the incumbent may decrease his quality investment to make it easier for the entrant to penetrate the market. Our model sheds light on entry scenarios of particular platform product markets such as the entry of Xbox to the video game console market.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

Quality can be broadly defined as any product attribute that increases end-user satisfaction and hence willingness to buy, such as reliability, service, or variety. Thus quality has emerged as a vital strategy in recent years and redefined the dynamics of competition. For example, since entering the US market, Toyota gained significant market share and overtook General Motors in sales to become the top automaker in the US, in large part due to Toyota’s reputation for reliability. However, when Toyota’s acceleration problems led to more than nine million recalls worldwide, the damage on the carmaker’s reputation and its impact on sales were significant; Toyota’s 2010 US sales declined while the competitors enjoyed a 13% gain (Bunkley, 2011). The role of quality is not limited to manufacturing; quality plays an important role in the service industry as well – this time under the rubric of “service.” Aggressive investments in customer satisfaction enabled Amazon.com to emerge as the market leader in e-commerce in early 2000s; the company offered superior service with its enhanced supply chain and distribution network (which enabled Amazon.com to offer free and fast shipping in 2002), customer review system, and recommendation engine (Casadesus-Masanell & Thaker, 2012).

Recently, similar dynamics to that of traditional quality/service problems have emerged in other settings. For example, in 2009, AT&T acquired 875,000 new customers in a single quarter, around 75% of which chose AT&T due to the company’s iPhone exclusivity agreement with Apple (Hansell, 2009). Although this exclusivity agreement required high subsidization, with expectations of 2.5 million new iPhone customers a year demanding expensive data plans, AT&T enjoyed growth in revenue and wireless operating margin (Hansell, 2009). AT&T’s iPhone exclusivity investment shares similar characteristics with a quality investment; it facilitated a demand spike and change in operating costs/savings.

Our definition of quality applies to some platform product

                      markets as well. For example, for a platform like a video game console, the availability and variety of compatible games with that console is a critical factor (Fackler, 2007; Powers, 2003; Wildstrom, 2005). After all, “games drive console sales” (Fujimura, 2013). Thus, to boost console sales, manufacturers manipulate game availability (i.e., variety) by developing their own games in-house and/or setting up license agreements with independent game developers/publishers (Hoyt, Lee, & Holloway, 2006). By these agreements, manufacturers also collect royalties from game sales (Coughlan, 2004). Development of a single game typically takes 18months and it could cost in excess of $10M (Black, 2001; Greene & Edwards, 2004). Hence, much like quality of a product, variety is a long-term investment. In terms of revenue, variety acts similar to quality: per each console sold, the manufacturer receives the console revenue and royalties from games. In fact, “Nintendo […] derives almost all its revenue from games” (Fujimura, 2013). Similar dynamics also apply to the emerging smart TV market where TV manufacturers escalate their efforts to increase available content and uncover new avenues of revenue generation from content consumption (Elliott, 2011; Hyong-ki, 2012; Turbow, 2010).

An intriguing question is whether quality (defined in broad terms by Kaya & Özer (2009) as “product attributes which consumers prefer more to less”) can also be used as an effective and credible entry-deterrence strategy. We study the role of quality in entry deterrence by considering a potential entry scenario to a monopoly market and we characterize the incumbent’s entry-deterrence strategies involving his quality investment. If entry occurs, the two firms compete by setting prices. We derive the conditions motivating the incumbent to blockade the entrant (i.e., prevent entry without over-investing in quality), deter the entrant (i.e., over-invest in quality to prevent entry), or accommodate the entrant (i.e., allow the entry to take place). We compare the use of manipulating quality with other strategies such as vertical differentiation, manipulating capacity (Dixit, 1980) or advertising (Schmalensee, 1983) in terms of over- and under-investments for deterrence and/or accommodation.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. We briefly discuss the relevant literature in Section 2, and discuss the model details in Section 3. In Section 4, we study our entry model and characterize conditions under which the incumbent blockades, deters, or accommodates the entrant. Finally, in Section 5, we discuss how our model extends to some platform product markets such as video game console markets and we conclude the paper. To improve the readability of the paper, all proofs are presented in Appendix A.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

The quality/service decisions of a firm have been the focus of a vast literature in operations management, marketing, and economics (e.g., Allon & Federgruen, 2007; André, González, & Porteiro, 2009; Banker, Khosla, & Sinha, 1998; Bernstein & Federgruen, 2004; Federgruen & Yang, 2009; Karmarkar & Pitbladdo, 1997; Lacourbe, Loch, & Kavadias, 2009; Matsubayashi, 2007; Moorthy, 1988; Nair & Narasimhan, 2006; Narasimhan & Mendez, 2001; Paulson Gjerde & Slotnick, 2004; Tsay & Agrawal, 2000; Yayla-Küllü, Parlaktürk, & Swaminathan, 2013). Different timelines (simultaneous versus dynamic) and relationships (competitive versus collaborative) have been analyzed to explain the role quality/service plays in competition intensity in the market, in coordination problems, in inventory and capacity decisions, in product differentiation and vertical integration of firms, etc. We contribute to these streams of literature by studying a situation in which an incumbent faces a potential entry and by investigating the incumbent’s entry-deterrence strategies in terms of quality decisions.

A monopolist’s reaction to a potential entry and his credible commitment to a strategy for deterrence, as we study in this paper, has been analyzed in different settings in the economics literature (Wilson, 1992, chap. 10). For example, Hung and Schmitt (1988), Donnenfeld and Weber (1992, 1995), Lehmann-Grube (1997), Peitz (2002), Lutz (1997), and Noh and Moschini (2006) study the use of quality as a vertical product differentiation strategy together with price decisions in various entry scenarios. Although these papers share our primary research questions, they model quality in a Hotelling vertical differentiation context, which drives the incumbent’s decisions. Additionally, as most of these papers do not consider the cost implications of quality, the tradeoff between the investment requirements and potential demand loss due to entry are not properly addressed. In this respect, Lutz (1997) and Noh and Moschini (2006) are the closest papers to our work; the former considers a quality cost independent of the production quantity and the latter a convex marginal cost of quality. Even in these papers, deterrence is possible with modest quality levels and consequently cost values due to the nature of the vertical differentiation; and hence, the incumbent’s strategy is mainly driven by the fixed entry cost. Contrary to these papers, we adopt the same approach as Banker et al. (1998), Tsay and Agrawal (2000), Matsubayashi (2007), Allon and Federgruen (2007), and treat the quality decision as a “demand-enhancing effort” (Tsay & Agrawal, 2000) in a non-address product differentiation model. A higher quality, ceteris paribus, always produces a higher demand, but has significant cost consequences – in both investment and marginal costs/savings. Thus, the investment incentives are properly aligned with the market gain and the tradeoff between costs and profits in an entry scenario are properly set up. In fact, our main insights revolve around the magnitude of quality-related costs. Therefore, our work sheds light on the quality investment problem from a different perspective – more as a tool to maintain or strengthen market dominance than vertical differentiation – and complements the extant literature.

Entry scenarios have been studied in various other settings. Spence (1977, 1979) and Dixit (1979, 1980) study capacity and capital investments as tools to deter entry and find that incumbents may in fact prevent entry by carrying excess capacity or by increasing their investment levels. Schmalensee (1978) demonstrates how product proliferation can be an effective entry deterrence strategy with an analysis inspired by the US ready-to-eat breakfast cereal industry. Schmalensee (1983) analyzes whether it is possible to make an analogy between the use of capital investments and advertising as an entry-deterrence tool. He finds that the incumbent may prefer to deter only by under-investment, and never by over-investment. Liu and Zhang (2006) and Liu, Gupta, and Zhang (2006) study entry-deterrence decisions in the context of e-tail and secondary markets, and Seamans (2012) demonstrates how private cable TV companies respond by technology upgrades to potential entry threats by public firms. Additionally, Xiao and Qi (2010) study how the incumbent supplier in a two-stage supply chain reacts to the potential entry of a second supplier through wholesale pricing, and Matsui (2012) shows how the integration of a firm’s manufacturing and marketing channels can be effective in deterring a potential entry. The literature on entry deterrence investigates how the motivation to deter an entry may induce the incumbent to behave in ways otherwise non-optimal. In this respect, the papers in this literature share our research goals but operate within different contexts and dynamics.

Faced with a potential competition, the incumbent may either let the entry take place or not. The incumbent may keep the entrant out of the market in two ways: he may blockade or deter the entry. @&#CONCLUSION@&#

Entry-deterrence strategies may not perform similarly in different settings. In a capacity context, capital over-investment may achieve better economies of scale and keep potential entrants out by leveraging a cost advantage in the market (Dixit, 1979, 1980; Spence, 1977, 1979). In an advertising context, however, over-investment increases the incumbent’s pricing vulnerability and the market attractiveness for the entrant; i.e., the incumbent may prefer to deter by under-investing (Schmalensee, 1983). When quality as a vertical differentiation strategy is studied in an entry scenario, vertical differentiation dynamics may overpower investment incentives and conflicting results may emerge. For example, Lutz (1997) finds that the incumbent will always deter when entry cost is high enough; deterrence happens with a quality level lower than that of monopoly. Moreover, an increase in the cost components will make deterrence more attractive and feasible (which contradicts with our finding that it is easier to penetrate markets of high quality-related investment costs). On the other hand, using a convex quality-related unit cost, Noh and Moschini (2006) find that the incumbent will over-invest in quality to both accommodate and deter the entrant. This variation in results is mainly due to the fact that vertical differentiation imposes intermediate levels of investments for deterrence, operational costs are not completely captured, and hence the tradeoff between quality cost and market gain are not properly addressed in these studies. In this paper, we analyze the use of quality as a pure “demand-enhancing effort” in a linear demand model of product differentiation and do not observe any of the counterintuitive results found in the past studies. We conclude that the incumbent may use both over-investment to deter and under-investment to accommodate entry depending on cost and market characteristics, as well as the additional market potential of duopoly. Thus, we fully portray the tradeoff between the investment and the demand generation side of quality.


                     Amazon.com and its effective use of Amazon Prime service is a good example for the dynamics we study in this paper. Consumers who subscribe to Prime receive free two-day shipping for their purchases in addition to free access to various video content on Amazon. Industry analysts suggest that with the convenience of Prime, members end up spending much more on Amazon (Grant, 2013). Thus, similar to our model, with a high service/quality Amazon not only increases its customer base but also achieves a higher revenue per member. In the past, Amazon used Prime effectively to deter entry; by rolling out Amazon Mom (an enhanced Prime membership to new parents), the company managed to discourage the niche entrant diapers.com and later acquired the company (Stone, 2013). Similarly, to deter the entry of the newcomers (such as Google Shopping Express, Instacart, and Good Eggs) and to compete more effectively with major competitors (such as eBay) in the grocery delivery market, Amazon accelerated its efforts to roll out AmazonFresh in major cities and developed a new premium membership called Amazon Prime Fresh (Frank, 2014; Tso, 2013). Hence, the use of Prime service by Amazon is a perfect example of what we refer to as quality in our study and it is an effective lever against entry and competition.

Our definition of quality extends beyond the traditional quality/service definition and applies to some platform product markets as well.
                        5
                        Therefore, our results contribute to the literature on platform products as well. We refer the interested reader to Rochet and Tirole (2003), Parker and Van Alstyne (2005), Hagiu (2006), Zhu and Iansiti (2012) for representative research on platform products. In particular, Zhu and Iansiti (2012) study an entry scenario in platform markets. However, the authors adopt the simultaneous arrival dynamics of both markets and focus on the impact of indirect network effects versus the quality levels of platforms. In contrast, our focus is on the platform manufacturers as decision makers.
                     
                     
                        5
                      For example, for a platform like a video game console, the availability and variety of compatible games with that console impact the attractiveness of the platform and drive its demand (Fackler, 2007; Powers, 2003; Wildstrom, 2005). Console manufacturers generally have full control on the games compatible with their product; only approved games can be published for a specific console with a royalty paid to the console maker for every game sold (Hagiu & Herman, 2013). As a good selection of games would boost game consumption, console manufacturers have the primary responsibility to invest in game availability (i.e., variety) – both for the sake of console and consequently for game sales.

With this new interpretation, we conclude that variety investment may become a critical lever in entry deterrence in the video game console market. The incumbent, depending on the market characteristics, may blockade, over-invest and deter, under-invest and accommodate, or simply accommodate a potential entrant. The entry of Sony (in 1994) and XBox (in 2000) to the game console market are two real-life examples of our entry scenario. The intimidating determination of Sony and Microsoft and the expensive and risky nature of game development positions this case as a low entry cost – high investment cost scenario. Our result agrees with the actual market outcomes in both cases; it suggests the Accommodate strategy for the incumbent where he is willing to accept the entry and invest for the duopoly market. However, the incumbents’ strategies in these two entry scenarios were different, leading to different experiences for the entrants.

When Sony entered the game console market in 1994, the incumbents (Nintendo and Sega) made a fatal error in limiting the number of games and underestimating the support of third-party game developers. Sony, on the other hand, made generous investments, on time: the company spent $48 million to buy Psygnosis, the creator of a hit game, and “gave third-party developers an unprecedented array of development tools and software libraries that made it easier to write games for the PlayStation” (Hagiu, 2007). By the time the PlayStation was launched, many game developers had already committed to develop games for the console, creating a surge in the number of PlayStation-compatible games. As a result of Sony’s smart entry strategy, by 1999, PlayStation sales reached 81 million, almost three times the sales of its closest competitor (Hagiu, 2007). One wonders how the console market would shape if Nintendo could have attracted more support from third-party developers and did not have to rely on in-house game development for 57% of its games.

When Microsoft decided to enter the game console market with Xbox, “one of its first stops was Electronic Arts’ [the leading game developer and publisher] headquarters” (Guth, 2003). Sony, however, did not make the mistake Nintendo and Sega had done, and better defended its market. The PlayStation2 was launched in 2000 and the new console was backward-compatible, which means it had a whole library of the PlayStation games that was compatible with the new console right at the start. As a result, Microsoft’s entry to the game console market was more painful; the company faced a 7-year stretch of losses from 2000 to 2006 totaling above $8 billion (Hagiu & Herman, 2013). One wonders what would happen if Sony had previously focused on game development capabilities both in-house and through third-party developers to achieve reduced costs over time, or had signed exclusivity agreements with big publishers like Electronic Arts in order to limit the investment opportunities of a potential entrant.

Our paper contributes to the literature in two ways. First, understanding how quality can be used as an entry-deterrence strategy is important, especially under the general quality definition that we propose here. Our model is a first step in addressing this question. Second, we argue that general quality models and entry scenarios could extend to dynamics beyond the quality context, such as in platform-content markets. We hope this observation will unlock a fresh perspective on the topic and inspire new avenues of research.
