@&#MAIN-TITLE@&#Outsource planning through option contracts with demand and cost uncertainty


@&#HIGHLIGHTS@&#


               
               
                  
                     
                        
                           
                           An outsourcing model considering both demand and cost uncertainty is investigated.


                        
                        
                           
                           Supply orders are analyzed according to wholesale price and option contracts.


                        
                        
                           
                           Closed form formulas are presented to determine optimal exercised options.


                        
                        
                           
                           Equations in which optimal initial and option orders hold are explored.


                        
                        
                           
                           The investigated model is applicable for seasonal and innovative products.


                        
                     
                  
               
            

@&#KEYPHRASES@&#

Supply chain management

Outsource planning

Option contract

Demand/Cost uncertainty

@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  This research considers a supply chain consisting of one supplier and one manufacturer that produces a kind of product, e.g. innovative products, with a long supply lead-time, a short selling season and a stochastic demand. Complete production of the final product requires both an initial and a final processing operation. The manufacturer performs the initial processing operation with a deterministic cost. The final processing operation may be either performed by the manufacturer or assigned to an outside firm through a bid process. At the time of the supply contract, the final processing cost (FPC) is estimated as a stochastic variable. The uncertainty on FPC is removed before the selling season starts. The present study is an attempt to determine how the manufacturer should place the supply orders within the framework of wholesale price, put, call and bidirectional options. Option contracts provide the manufacturer with the flexibility to adjust his initial orders by exercising purchased options after the FPC is realized. We find optimal exercised orders with each option contract, in addition to equations in which the optimal initial and option orders hold. According to our analysis, if the realized FPC is higher (lower) than a specific level, the manufacturer should decrease (increase) his initial orders. We obtain analytically these specific levels under all types of option contracts. The numerical analysis and managerial insights shed light on the value of option contracts considering different parameter settings.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

There are several practical motivations for investigating outsource planning under information uncertainty such as demand or cost uncertainty. Long supply lead times generally occur with industries that are involved in the production of innovative products such as apparel, toys, consumer electronics, personal computers, and seasonal products (Liu, Chen, Li, & Zhai, 2014; Wang, Chu, Wangc, & Kumakiri, 2012). In such industries, outsourcing decisions, like supply orders, should be made well in advance of the selling season with stochastic demands. This is while at the time of placing supply orders, especially when the technology is new or when a new manufacturer enters into the market, it is difficult to evaluate the technology, development, and processing costs or even the selling price. Thus, there is some uncertainty with some of the information, such as demand or processing costs in the next stages, that affects outsourcing. Predicting such information is extremely difficult, if not impossible, making outsourcing decisions even more complicated to the extent that serious consideration and in-depth analysis will be required.

Another practical motivation for investigating outsourcing in cases of uncertainty comes from manufacturing firms that discharge part of their processing operations to an outside firm due to lack of production capacity or expertise. Usually, the outside firm is selected through a bidding process, in which potential bidders submit their bid proposals. One of the most important features of proposals from the viewpoint of the client manufacturing firm is the cost of processing operations by the successful contractor. This cost which accounts for part of the expenses borne by the manufacturing firm toward a final product remains unknown until the bidding process is finalized and the winning contractor is selected. On the other hand, the client manufacturing firm usually has to decide on how the raw materials are to be supplied before the bidding documents are prepared. The winning contractors’ cost proposal may be so high that the business becomes non-profitable for the client manufacturing firm. Thus, the processing costs proposed by the potential contractor is a type of uncertain information that has a direct bearing on the manufacturer's profit and, thereby, on decisions about outsourcing. Real world cases for such situations come from companies that make use of procurement auctions, such as SUN Microsystems, Hewlett–Packard (HP), IBM, Samsung, and Lucent (Hohner et al., 2003; Morales & Steinberg, 2014).

An appropriate ordering policy for outsourcing in cases where there is an information uncertainty is to use option contracts. These contracts usually have two stages: an initial order is placed to be readjusted at a second stage. According to this type of contract, at the time of placing a supply order, a firm can purchase options in addition to initial orders in order to obtain the flexibility of adjusting its orders later. Depending on the type of option contract (i.e., put, call, or bidirectional), the firm with updated information decides about the options to be exercised. A put (call) option contract provides the firm with the flexibility to decrease (increase) its initial order while a bidirectional one provides the flexibility to either decrease or increase the initial order. Zhao, Ma, Xie, and Cheng, (2013) emphasized that bidirectional options are the preferable and the more complete form of option contracts. Such contracts have been extensively used in various industries such as fashion apparel, toys, and electronics (Barnes-Schuster, Bassok, & Anupindi, 2002; Eppen & Iyer, 1997; Zhao, Wang, Cheng, Yang, & Huang, 2010).

In this study, regarding the practical cases mentioned, we considered a two echelon supply chain consisting of one supplier and one manufacturer. The manufacturer performs a preprocessing and a final processing operation on the outsourced materials. The final processing operation may be performed by an outside firm as well. The cost of the preprocessing operation is deterministic while the FPC is uncertain at the time of the supply contract. The uncertainty with FPC is removed after some time, usually before the selling season starts. Once the selling season has started, the stochastic market demand for the manufacturer's products emerges. Thus, the manufacturer faces the two uncertainties of market demand and FPC at the time of outsourcing. Flexible supply contracts with options have been proposed to such firms with long lead-times, short selling seasons, and high demand uncertainties (Wang et al., 2012). The contracts are beneficial to both parties. In order to effectively respond to lean production and retailing, suppliers need to rely on flexible production (Abernathy, Dunlop, Hammond, & Weil, 2000). Thus, in this research, we focus on option contracts.

Our study has two main contributions. First, we develop an outsourcing model for the manufacturer to see how he should place supply orders in cases where both FPC and customer's demand are uncertain. Along this line, we develop different types of option contracts to reduce the impact of cost uncertainty, while demand uncertainty is incorporated into the model as a regular newsvendor problem. In addition, the model thus developed has the ability to be used for the case of price uncertainty instead of FPC uncertainty. Second, we determine optimal supply order quantities for the manufacturer under all option contracts and shed light on the value of option contracts considering different parameter settings in our illustrative numerical analysis.

Further, we investigate the manufacturer's decisions in two stages under call, put and bidirectional options. In the first stage, the manufacturer has to make decisions on the initial and option orders so as to maximize his expected profit. In the second stage, when the manufacturer realizes the exact value of FPC, he should decide how much of the options available to him should be exercised. After exercising the options, the manufacturer performs a preprocessing operation on the outsourced materials and prepares them for the selling season. We explore analytically closed form formulas for the optimal options exercised. According to our analysis, if the realized FPC is higher (lower) than a specific level, the manufacturer should decrease (increase) his initial orders. We will obtain analytically these specific levels under all option contracts. Also, equations are obtained in which optimal initial and option orders hold. Managerial insight into the value of option contracts are highlighted by a numerical analysis.

This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, the studies relevant to our research are reviewed. In Section 3, the problem and its assumptions are described. Section 4 investigates the supply orders according to wholesale price and different types of option contracts. Section 5 includes the numerical analysis and managerial insights. Finally, conclusions are presented in Section 6.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

This paper mainly deals with outsource planning and option contracts. The most relevant studies to this research are reviewed below.

The literature is rich in studies on outsource planning problems, especially those with a demand uncertainty. See for example Kouvelis and Milner (2002); Yang, Yang, and Abdel-Malek (2007); Jiang, Yao, and Feng (2008) and Jornsten, Nonas, Sandal, and Ubøe (2013) proposed a real option approach to evaluating the outsourcing contract under cost uncertainty from the vendors’ perspective. They investigated the problem of determining the best time when the vendor should exercise his options and considered variable operating costs that evolve over time as a general Brownian motion. Focusing on service processes, Alvarez and Stenbacka (2007) and Benaroch, Webster, and Kazaz (2012) developed flexible sourcing models to determine the expected time of a switch to outsourcing. They considered dynamic models in which the outsourced cost per transaction is variable. Benaroch et al. (2012) also considered fixed overhead costs in their model. In these studies, the considered variable cost per process transaction depends on time, while in our case the variable cost is treated as a stochastic parameter from a probabilistic viewpoint.

Some authors (Inderfurth & Kele, 2011; Spinler & Huchzermeier, 2006) investigated outsource planning problems with both demand and cost uncertainties. In these studies, cost/price uncertainty belongs to a seller (i.e., the upstream member in the supply chain) or spot markets. However, in our model, cost uncertainty belongs to the downstream member in the supply chain (i.e., the manufacturer). In addition to facility capacity uncertainty, Bidhandi and Yusuff (2011) considered operational cost and demand uncertainties in a model developed from the perspective of the supply chain network design. Liu and Nagurney (2013) proposed a model for supply chain networks with global outsourcing and quick-response production. Considering demand and cost uncertainties, their analysis is based on the variational inequality theory in which some scenarios are considered for demand and quick-response production costs.

As already mentioned, studies on option contracts can be classified into one of the three groups of put, call, or bidirectional options. While several studies have been devoted to the application of put options (Chen & Parlar, 2007; Donohue, 2000; Granot & Yin, 2005; Song, Ray, & Li, 2008; Yang & Qi, 2009), others have focused on call options (Barnes-Schuster et al., 2002; Burnetas & Ritchken, 2005; Buzacott, Yan, & Zhang, 2011; Nosoohi & Nookabadi, 2014; Wang & Liu, 2007; Wang, Li, Liang, Huang, & Ashley, 2015). Unlike these studies, we additionally consider bidirectional options.

A few studies have dealt with the implementation of bidirectional options. Milner and Rosenblatt (2002) modeled a flexible supply contract for which penalties are charged for bidirectional adjustments made to the initial order. The adjustments are made based on information gained about demand and inventory position. They demonstrated how changes in the adjustment penalties affect initial orders. Wang and Tsao (2006) later developed a supply contract with bidirectional options in which a buyer updates its demand forecast. In contrast to Milner and Rosenblatt (2002), Wang and Tsao (2006) limited adjustment on the range of orders. Gomezpadilla and Mishina (2009) analyzed a dynamic model based on bidirectional options under two structures where there is one retailer and either one or multiple suppliers. In their study, market demand is considered as a non-stationary stochastic process. They used simulation to compare the increased benefits for the supply chain members in the case of an option contract over wholesale and buy-back contracts. While these studies mainly considered the role of updated demand information, we focus on updated cost/profit information. Zhao et al. (2013) studied a supply contract with the bidirectional option between a manufacturer and a retailer. They examined the feedback effects of the bidirectional option on the retailer's initial order strategy. The major difference between their study and ours is that they investigated bidirectional options from the viewpoint of the upstream supply chain member, whereas the analysis in the present study adopts the viewpoint of the downstream supply chain member. Moreover, their bidirectional model considered an additional charge for exercising call options exactly equal to the refund obtained from exercising put options. This assumption limits the application of the bidirectional model since these two parameters might not be the same in practice. In our study, similar to Wang and Tsao (2006), we consider separate parameters for exercising call or put options.

Similar to Milner and Rosenblatt (2002) and also Wang and Tsao (2006), the analytical procedure employed in the present work is based on the perspective from the downstream member in the supply chain. However, the model considered in this paper is different from theirs in that cost uncertainty was not considered in their work in combination with demand uncertainty. Moreover, we analyze and compare the manufacturer's decisions numerically based on put, call and, bidirectional options to highlight both the differences among these flexible contracts and the value of bidirectional options.

We consider a two-echelon supply chain consisting of one supplier and one manufacturer. The manufacturer outsources his required materials from the supplier and transfers them to a final product after two processing operations. For simplicity, we assume that each unit of the final product requires one unit of the outsourced materials. The manufacturer performs a primary processing operation (e.g., blending and forming operations) on all the outsourced materials. This is a case of preprocessing operation with a considerable lead-time. A final processing (e.g., assembling and packaging) operation is also performed before the final product is delivered to customers. Each of the preprocessing and final processing operations may include several stages.

It should be mentioned that, considering two processing operations and the related costs in our model is an attempt to generalize our model and to capture the situation of firms that perform part of their processes in-house and, for any reason like the lack of capacity or expertise, pay an outside firm to perform a final processing operation. It is clear that ignoring the primary processing operation and the related cost leads to a specific case of our model in which the manufacturer produces a kind of product, e.g. innovative products, just with one processing operation.

We further suppose that the manufacturer, because of long supply lead time, has to place supply orders well in advance of the selling season. The manufacturer cannot place any other orders to the supplier after the selling season has started. These are the regular assumptions for innovative and fashion products with long supply lead-times, short selling seasons, and uncertain demands.

It is further assumed that the manufacturer does not know the exact FPC at the time of the supply contract. This is a realistic assumption for innovative products, especially when the technology employed is new. This situation is also observed when a bid process should be held in order to realize the FPC proposed by an outside firm. Under each case, the manufacturer realizes FPC as time passes. It is assumed that the FPC uncertainty will be later removed before the selling season starts. In this situation, we may suppose that the manufacturer has access to a probability density function from FPC at the time of the supply contract. In order to avoid some trivial cases, in which the business becomes non-profitable for the manufacturer, it is assumed that FPC cannot be greater than the selling price. It is obvious that the manufacturer's optimal supply orders and profit will otherwise become zero.

One further assumption is that the time required for the final processing operation is acceptable to customers. Therefore, in order to eliminate the extra inventory of finished products, the final process on the product is performed after the stochastic market demand is realized. This assumption helps us use our model for cases with uncertain selling price as well. In this sense, we are considering a fixed part as the maximum value of selling price and a stochastic part that reduces the value of the fixed part.

No correlation is assumed between distributions of demand and FPC. Also, similar to Wang and Tsao (2006) and Wang et al. (2012), we suppose that in the business relation between the supplier and the manufacturer, the supplier offers a pricing mechanism to the manufacturer and the manufacturer places orders with the supplier accordingly.

In this situation, we need to determine how the manufacturer facing uncertain information about FPC and market demand should place his supply orders. As mentioned above, one supply contract appropriate for the manufacturer in this situation is the option contract. It should be mentioned that while we are considering both demand and cost uncertainties, we are using the option contract to reduce the effects of cost uncertainty, rather than demand uncertainty, since FPC is updated in our model.

@&#CONCLUSION@&#

Previous studies on outsource planning have mainly focused on the role of updated demand information and ignored the role of updated cost/profit information. In the model developed in this study, we shed light on the importance and significance of updated cost information for the downstream member of the supply chain (i.e., the manufacturer). At the time of placing supply orders, the manufacturer faces both demand and FPC uncertainties. However, the FPC uncertainty is removed after some time. Option contracts provide the manufacturer with the flexibility to change his initial order after realizing the exact value of FPC. According to the analysis in the bidirectional option, which is the complete form of the put and call options, the manufacturer has the opportunity to increases (or decreases) his initial orders if the realized FPC is too low (or too high). Based on Property Five, different ranges were analytically found for FPC, in which put, zero, or call options should be exercised.

The numerical analysis highlighted the profitability of option contracts compared to the wholesale price contract under different parameter settings. We observed that option contracts were more valuable for the manufacturer than the wholesale price contract, especially when w, ci, μC, σC
                      increase or when wo, p decrease. Also, among the options, the bidirectional case was found to be always more profitable than the put or the call option. How the latter two options compare mostly depends on the different model parameters employed. The results show that when demand and FPC variations increase, the call option is more valuable for the manufacturer than the put option.

It must be noted that, while the model developed here captures the uncertainty for the manufacturer's FPC, it can also be interpreted as considering uncertainty for the final price. In this sense, the final price of the product is 
                        
                           p
                           −
                           
                              c
                              f
                           
                        
                     , which includes a fixed part p and a variable part cf
                     .

One limitation with our study is that we analyzed only the manufacturer's decisions. Other researchers (e.g., Wang & Liu, 2007; Zhao et al., 2010; Zhao et al., 2013) focusing on the supply chain perspective investigated the supply chain coordination based on option contracts. However, it was assumed in these studies that the contract designer has access to all cost and profit information of its option contract partner at the time of designing the contract. In this situation, the contract designer could design a contract to coordinate its partner's decisions by simply adjusting the parameters of the option contract. However, the manufacturer's FPC in our analysis is not known to the supplier at the time of designing the supply contract and, as a result, the supplier cannot have a clear understanding of his partner's ordering decisions. Based on our analysis, we cannot present any analytically closed form relation for the manufacturer's initial and option orders in any of the put, call, or bidirectional options. Thus, the supplier in our study cannot perform a parameter adjustment to handle the manufacturer's ordering decisions. Nevertheless, our investigations indicate that the supplier can analyze the manufacturer's reaction to any option contracts. Therefore, our analysis can be helpful from the supplier's perspective for choosing optimal parameters in the option contracts when there are some finite option contracts to choose from.
