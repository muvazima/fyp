@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  We observe that the laborious collection of location fingerprints, that could also potentially change with time and space, remains a hurdle towards the widespread deployment of indoor and outdoor positioning systems using the Wi-Fi. Motivated by this, we focus on the error bound analysis of indoor Wi-Fi fingerprint based positioning for intelligent Access Point (AP) placement optimization by using the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to characterize the relationship between the positioning errors and signal distributions. We first derive the closed-form error bounds with respect to different system parameters. Second, we use the Simulated Annealing (SA) algorithm to conduct the Wi-Fi AP placement optimization towards the lowest error bounds. Finally, we present the detailed discussion, extensive simulation, and experiment results.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

In recent decade, there has been a growing interest in indoor positioning technology that relies on the existing indoor high-speed wireless access networks [1], like the Wi-Fi, Zigbee, and Radio Frequency Identification (RFID). Compared to the conventional trilateration based positioning approach which is vulnerable to the large-scale path loss, multi-path effect, and shadow fading, the location fingerprint based positioning approach [2,3] has been preferred. In Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning, a batch of Reference Points (RPs) are first calibrated in the target area. Then, the location fingerprint which is typically a vector of the averaged Received Signal Strengths (RSSs) from the hearable Access Points (APs) is collected at each RP. The set of location fingerprints and the corresponding physical coordinates is named as a radio map. The process of radio map construction becomes significantly time consuming and labor intensive as the dimensions of the target area increase. After the radio map is constructed, for each location query, we match the newly collected RSS against the radio map to report the estimated location of the receiver.

Due to the existence of the multi-path effect, shadow fading, and channel interference in indoor Wi-Fi environment, there are significant temporal and spatial variations of RSSs [4–6]. Up to now, there are very few literatures focusing on the theoretical analysis of the errors with respect to the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning, but in fact, the error analysis is very important to the design of the effective indoor Wi-Fi positioning system. Motivated by this, we propose a theoretical model which considers the Gaussian, Rayleigh, and Rice signal distributions in characterizing the variation of RSSs to derive the closed-form error bounds of the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning. Furthermore, the error bounds can be used to address the Wi-Fi AP placement optimization problem.

The four main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows. First of all, the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) is used to derive the error bounds of the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning. Second, the theoretical analysis towards the relationship between the positioning errors and signal distributions including the Gaussian, Rayleigh, and Rice signal distributions is presented. Third, due to the difference of signal distributions corresponding to different Wi-Fi APs, the error bound with respect to the mixed signal distribution is considered. Fourth, the Wi-Fi AP placement are optimized towards the lowest error bounds.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 gives some related work on the error bounds of Wi-Fi positioning. In Section 3, we derive the error bounds of the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning, as well as conduct the Wi-Fi AP placement optimization for the sake of achieving the lowest error bounds. The extensive simulations are provided in Section 4. Some interesting discussions are shown in Section 5. Finally, we conclude the paper and provide the future directions in Section 6.

@&#RELATED WORK@&#

In the past decade, the Wi-Fi fingerprint based localization precision has been studied extensively. The authors in [32] proposed that the number and locations of APs, physical layout, and mean of RSSs at RPs have significant impact on localization precision. To optimize the AP placement, the authors in [29] proposed a novel approach by using a small number of APs to provide full coverage while locating the mobile device within an area with limited size. Wang and Lin [30] proposed a goal programming-driven model which is intergraded with a genetic algorithm and an embedded mask mechanism to resolve the problems of multiple objective AP deployment construction and enhancement. The algorithm proposed in [31] regards the location discriminant information and degree of unstable measurements as the signal and noise respectively. Based on this, the purpose of the approach in [31] aims at finding the optimal set of APs by which the signal is maximized and the noise is minimized simultaneously. In addition, the authors in [27] invented a weighted kernel function by which the impact of different APs is differentiated. In [28], the authors proposed the max Euclidean Distance (ED) based AP placement optimization approach, aiming to achieve the maximal sum of ED between every two location fingerprints. The Localized Local Discriminant Embedding and AP Selection (LLDE-APS) approach in [28] not only optimizes the AP placement, but also saves the power consumed by the mobile terminal. The approach in [28] is featured with the significant advantage of extracting the most discriminative RSS features for localization.

The existing studies on the error bounds of Wi-Fi positioning can be generally classified into two categories. The first one is about the error bounds with respect to the trilateration based positioning, while the second one is about the error bounds with respect to the location fingerprint based positioning. These two categories are described respectively as follows.

There are a batch of studies focusing on the error bounds of positioning in Wireless Sensor Networks (WSNs) by using the FIM. As one of the most representative studies on the error bounds of the trilateration based positioning, the authors in [7] introduced the Cramer–Rao Lower Bound (CRLB) to calculate the positioning precision based on the prior statistics of the errors resulted from the None-line-of-sight (NLOS) property. Furthermore, they also investigated that the proper utilization of the multipath delay can help a lot in improving the accuracy of the Time of Arrival (TOA) based positioning. The authors in [8] used the CRLB to estimate the sensor locations by conducting the unbiased Gaussian range estimation based on the anchor nodes in the Angle of Arrival (AOA) based positioning. Similarly, the authors in [9] achieved the highly-accurate sensor positioning by using the RSS and TOA measurements. In [10], the authors analyzed the basic invariance of the Cramer–Rao Bound (CRB), as well as derived the lower and upper CRBs. However, the singular FIM problem always results in the significant complication of the CRB. To solve this problem, the authors in [11] proposed an approach that equals the calculation of the CRB to the solution to an unconstrained quadratic maximization problem with low computation complexity. The authors in [12] developed the information inequality based Position Error Bound (PEB) for the TOA based cooperative positioning.

Using the nonparametric kernel density estimation approach, the authors in [13] estimated the Probability Density Functions (PDFs) of errors and the corresponding confidence regions for the generalized location fingerprint based positioning. The authors in [14] found that the Signal Strength Difference (SSD) can be used to generate more robust location fingerprints than the ones generated from the RSSs. This result is proved by the lower CRLB of errors for the SSD based positioning compared to the RSS based positioning in [15]. Although the FIM has been widely used for error bounds analysis in WSNs [16–18], the existing literatures paid very little attention to the theoretical relationship between the positioning errors and signal distributions in the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning. Therefore, the error bounds analysis under different signal distributions in Wi-Fi environments remains an open problem. To solve this problem, we select the FIM to conduct the analysis on the relationship between the error bounds and different signal distributions, like the Gaussian, Rayleigh, Rice, and mixed signal distributions. After that, the error bounds are applied to optimize the AP placement with the lowest error bounds.

In this paper, we proposed the intelligent AP placement optimization system. We first use all the candidate AP locations in the target area to construct the solution space for the AP placement optimization problem. Second, considering the physical distances from different APs to the receiver, we divide the target area into three subareas in which the RSSs follow the Gaussian, Rayleigh, and Rice signal distributions respectively. Third, we derive the error bounds of the Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning under the Gaussian, Rayleigh, Rice, and mixed signal distributions respectively. Finally, we conduct the Wi-Fi AP placement optimization with the purpose of achieving the lowest error bounds. In general, our system consists of three main modules: the (i) division of target area; (ii) derivation of the error bounds of Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning; and (iii) AP placement optimization. Hence, the discussion of the characteristics of indoor Wi-Fi RSSs is essential for the analysis of error bounds. In the results that follow, we assume that: the (i) signal phases follow the uniform distribution; and (ii) signal phases and amplitudes are independent from each other.

To address the computation complexity problem, we apply the Simulated Annealing (SA) algorithm to search for the optimal AP locations when the AP number is larger than 4.

As one of the most representative heuristic optimization algorithms, the SA algorithm [24,25] applies the concept of the annealing process in metallurgy to conduct the optimization searching. Specifically, in the SA algorithm, the initial temperature is first set before the annealing process. Then, as the temperature drops, this algorithm searches for the optimal solutions in the iterative manner. Different from most existing optimization algorithms, the SA algorithm distributes the acceptance probability to the new solution in each iteration. Furthermore, the SA algorithm has been proved to be able to consume much lower time overhead compared to the widely used brute-force optimization algorithms [26].

@&#DISCUSSION@&#

There are still two interesting questions that remain to be answered. The first one is about the difference of the AP placement optimization and the corresponding positioning errors under the Gaussian, Rayleigh, Rice, and mixed signal distributions. The second one is about the performance comparison between the proposed SA algorithm and other existing optimization algorithms used for the optimal AP locations searching.


                        Figs. 14 and15 show the results of the optimal AP placement under the Gaussian, Rayleigh, Rice, and mixed signal distributions in the LOS and NLOS environments respectively. From these figures, we can find that the optimal AP locations which are corresponding to the lowest error bounds are non-collinearly distributed. To illustrate this result, by taking the mixed signal distribution as an example, we connect every two most adjacent optimal AP locations with a purple line. If the optimal AP locations are collinearly distributed, the obtained purple lines should also be collinear.


                        Figs. 16 and17 compare the CDFs of errors under the Gaussian, Rayleigh, Rice, and mixed signal distributions in the LOS and NLOS environments respectively. As can be seen from these figures, we find that the Gaussian signal distribution can generally achieve the smallest positioning errors, whereas the largest positioning errors are resulted by the Rayleigh signal distribution. Therefore, we can make a reasonable conjecture that the small positioning errors can be provided when the APs used for the location fingerprint based positioning are adjacent to the receiver.

We compare the CDFs of errors, as well as time overhead involved in the optimal AP locations searching by using the proposed SA algorithm and other two widely used optimization algorithms, the genetic and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) algorithms, in Fig. 18 and Fig. 19 respectively. From these figures, we observe that as the AP number increases, although the time overhead of the proposed SA algorithm increases slightly faster compared to the genetic and PSO algorithms, the proposed SA algorithm performs much better in positioning errors.

From this figure, we can find that the time overhead by the proposed approach is significantly lower than the one by LLED-APS or max ED approach since the latter two require traversing all the RPs in each round during the iterative process.

@&#CONCLUSION@&#

We present some preliminary analysis on the error bounds of the indoor Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning which can be recognized as the first step towards the better understanding of the relationship between the positioning errors and signal distributions in indoor Wi-Fi environments. The purpose of this analysis is to design an effective and efficient indoor Wi-Fi location fingerprint based positioning system, either through the judicious selection of the number and geometric locations of APs under different signal distributions, or through the reduction in the labor for searching for the optimal AP locations with the lowest error bounds. Furthermore, compared to the conventional max ED based and symmetric AP placement optimization approaches, the proposed approach performs better in both the positioning errors and time overhead. For the future work, the error bound analysis of the indoor Wi-Fi hybrid RSS, AOA, and TDOA based positioning for intelligent AP optimization by using the Fisher information forms an interesting topic.
