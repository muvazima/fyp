@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  We study a multiple period discount problem for products that undergo several price cuts over time. In the high-technology sector, electronic component suppliers are often able to offer pre-announced price cuts to buyers due to technological innovation that allows them to produce existing components at lower costs. In this context, suppliers are primarily concerned with the optimal pricing decisions for the components over their life spans in order to achieve the highest possible revenues. Accordingly, the buying firms (i.e., manufacturers or retailers) also need to identify the corresponding optimal retail prices and order quantity for the finished products that utilize the components for which discounts are offered frequently. In this research, we develop a multiple-period price discount model that addresses this issue. Extant research in the price discount literature focuses on supply chains’ pricing and inventory decisions in the presence of a single price discount. The proposed model, in contrast, offers a systematic decision tool for identifying the optimal strategies throughout a product's life span. Our results show that a decentralized supply chain characterized by multi-period discounts over a product's life typically achieves 75 percent supply chain efficiency. We undertake a series of numerical experiments based on the model and discuss their managerial implications.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

Price discount is a fundamental strategy in different types of businesses including the consumer packaged goods sector (breakfast cereals, dairy products), the service sector (cell phone service, and others), and the high-technology sector (PC assemblers) among others. Price discount problems exist in various contexts. In the personal computer (PC) assembly sector of the PC industry, electronic component suppliers are often able to develop newer components with higher speed or better performance due to technological innovation, which also allows them to produce existing components at lower costs (Lee, Padmanabhan, Taylor, & Whang, 2000). An excellent example is Intel's central processing unit (CPU) price-breaks (see Fig. 1
                     ). Intel reduces CPU prices approximately once a quarter; these price reductions are normally announced in advance. When the new price becomes effective, PC makers pay a lower price for the CPUs, regardless of the purchase quantity. PC makers then reduce the price of PCs in responding to CPU price cuts offered by Intel. Besides Intel, many other electronic component manufacturers, such as Seagate, Western Digital (Hard drive disks), Hitachi, Sony (optical drives), Micron, Hynix, Samsung (memory modules), and LG/Philips (LCD) also offer pre-announced price discounts due to technological innovation regularly (Chung, Narasimhan, & Talluri, 2012). As a result, the retail prices of PCs continually decrease over time. Importantly, consumers are, in general, unaware of such price reductions on PCs in advance. They normally learn about PC price reductions after the new, lower prices become effective through retailers’ advertisements in newspaper or surfing on retailers’ websites.

In this research, we study a problem involved with multiple price discounts over a product's life that is common in the high-tech sector. Generally, electronic components enter the market initially as high-end products commanding premium prices. However, over time, their prices gradually decrease, as newer, more advanced products replace them in the market. They eventually become a low-end product towards the end of their life cycles, and subsequently will be phased out of the market completely (Fig. 2).

In this context, electronic component suppliers are primarily concerned with the optimal price discount schemes for the components over their life span, a series of critical decisions for achieving the maximum possible profit. Accordingly, buying firms (e.g., manufacturers or retailers) also need to identify a series of corresponding optimal retail prices for the finished products in order to achieve the highest profit. Demand for a product may increase when firms adjust (reduce) the price in the early stages of its life. Therefore, another key decision that buying firms have to make is to the optimal order/production quantity in each period to cope with demand stimulated by price cuts. Understanding these issues allows supply chain managers to better respond to such instances. Extant price discount research mostly focuses on supply chain members’ price or/and inventory decisions in the presence of a single price discount (Bernstein, Chen, & Federgruen, 2006; Chung et al., 2012; Erhun, Keskinocak, & Tayur, 2008; Hu & Munson, 2002; Khouja, 1995; Weng, 1995). Our research, in contrast, considers the supplier's and buyer's price and inventory decisions throughout a product's life, giving rise to a multiple-period price discount problem.

Demand of a product that has relatively short life will not only be affected by its prices but also by the innovative position relative to other products in the market. Several demand functions that describe the patterns of product life cycle have been proposed in the literature (Aytac & Wu, 2013; Solomon, Sandborn, & Pecht, 2000), most of which are generated numerically by adopting the simulation approach. Although our research intends to study the pricing and inventory decisions of a product across multiple stages in its life, modeling product life cycles is not within the scope of this study. Rather, we incorporate such patterns in numerical experiments by adopting representative parameter values to mimic demand patterns in different stages of a product's life.

The remainder of this article is organized as follows. We first review literature related to the focus of our study in Section 2. We present the problem settings and formulate the multiple-period price markdown model in Section 3 and develop the optimal pricing and ordering decisions for the buyer and the supplier in Section 4. We further investigate the efficiency of such a decentralized supply chain and identify room for improvement in Section 5. We provide numerical examples and discuss managerial implications in Section 6. Finally, we conclude this research and suggest future research opportunities in Section 7. All proofs are shown in
Appendix.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

Price discount is one of the most commonly used strategies that can effectively influence demand levels (i.e., demand increases as price is decreased). It can also allow firms to potentially lower their inventory costs (i.e., price is cheaper when buying more). Interest in price discount problem continues unabated (Heydari, 2014; Huang, Su, & Lin, 2011; Luo, Leg, Huang, & Liang, 2014; Manerba & Mansini, 2012
                     , 
                     2014). Specifically, three different discount structures have been studied extensively in literature; they are all-units discount (Altintas, Erhun, & Tayur, 2008; Bernstein et al., 2006; Khouja, 1995), incremental quantity discount (Erhun et al., 2008; Hu & Munson, 2002), and price markdown (Dong & Zhu, 2007; Wee & Yu, 1997). All-units discount requires that the buyer pays the same unit price for every unit ordered with the unit price being determined by the quantity range into which the order fits (Altintas et al., 2008; Madan, Bramorski, & Gnanendran, 1993). Incremental discount offers a cheaper price only to the additional units ordered beyond a specified quantity (Gupta, 1988). In contrast, the buyer pays the same unit price regardless of ordering quantity when the supplier offers the price markdown scheme.

In each of the scenarios summarized above, there are some common and overarching themes associated with the discount problems that seem to unify the research from a supply chain's perspective. These include: (1) suppliers offer price discount incentives in the hope of stimulating sales volume, (2) excess inventories lead to high carrying costs and obsolescence, (3) buying organizations wish to manage procurement spend, and (4) buying organizations tend to place large special orders when offered price-cuts by suppliers (Ramasesh & Rachamadugu, 2001). In general, the main challenges are to find the optimal discount prices and order quantities that maximize a supply chain member's or the entire chain's total profits.

In economics, marketing, procurement and inventory management literatures, several researchers have developed a variety of models to study the effectiveness of price-break strategies. The economics and marketing literature (Allenby, Shively, Yang, & Garratt, 2004; Dolan, 1987) is diverse, but has tended to focus on issues of consumer utility or brand substitution and demand management. The purchasing and inventory management literature (Benton & Park, 1996; Tsay, Nahmias, & Agrawal, 1999) has equally diverse foci including: (1) buyer or seller perspective, (2) single vs. multiple products, and (3) single vs. multiple buyers (or sellers).

The discount problem we study in this research can be categorized as a price markdown problem. Research in this area is rich and abundant. For example, Gutpa, Hill, and Bouzdine-Chameeva (2006) studied a problem of price markdowns for clearing retail inventory of fashion goods. Chao, Chen, and Zheng (2008) studied a joint optimization problem of replenishment and pricing for a periodic-review inventory system with random supply capacity. Urban and Baker (1997) investigated an inventory model in which the demand for the product is a deterministic, multivariate function of price, time, and level of inventory, with or without a price markdown. Kurata, Yao, and Liu (2007) analyzed channel pricing in multiple distribution channels under competition between a national brand and a store brand.


                     Chung, Talluri, and Narasimhan (2011) studied a price markdown problem in a three-echelon supply chain, including a supplier, a contract manufacturer, and a retailer. They found that higher production cost accelerates the effects of higher price sensitivity on lowering the optimal capacity and stocking policies in the supply chain. They also found that increasing the standard deviation of the random demand does not necessarily increase the stocking level as one would predict. In a similar vein, Chung et al. (2012) investigated how the interplay of the buying firm's operating hours and the transportation carrier's delivery hours may affect a manufacturing firm's ending inventory decisions in the presence of a price cut. They found that stockout cost plays a crucial role in such decisions. However, in both research they only focused on a single period, although the discount price markdown problem they studied may undergo several discounts over multiple periods. Banerjee and Meitei (2010) studied a stochastic inventory problem observed from the fashion apparel business in which the retailer has only one ordering opportunity and the arrival lead-time is uncertain. Further, the selling price of the product is declining over time by the end of the selling season. They show that the optimal ordering policy is significantly different when the random lead-time and declined selling price are considered. Nonetheless, the product price is declining within one single selling season in their study.

Unlike the vast majority of single period price markdown problems studied in literature, this research investigates a multiple period price markdown problem that is widely observed in the high-technology sector. We develop a decision tool that can assist supply chain managers to better understand the effect of price markdowns on the optimal pricing and ordering policies. Similar to most work done in the supply chain contract literature (Altintas et al., 2008; Corbett & Groote, 2000; Wang, 2005; Weng, 1995), the price discount scheme that we consider in this paper follows the newsvendor problem framework. While many studies seek to design discount contracts that may coordinate a supply chain, this may not be practical in a decentralized supply chain where supply chain members act in their own interests (Wang, 2005).

More specifically, the proposed model focuses on electronic products that have relatively short shelf lives and that undergo several price discounts in their life span. As a result, our model meshes well with the well-known concept of product life cycle. Although anchored in the experiences of consumer electronics industry, the proposed model will be useful in other industries such as the consumer product industry, enhancing its applicability. Setting the right prices and buying the right quantity throughout a product's life span have been, and will always be, critical for achieving the desired profit levels for supply chain members. Research that considers price discount problems across various stages of a product's life cycle is still lacking. Our research seeks to fill this important gap in the current literature.

We consider a supply chain where a manufacturer (a buyer) purchases electronic components from a supplier. The supplier offers several price cuts (i.e., price markdown) for this component throughout its life span. These price-cuts are announced in advance and are applicable to each unit regardless of the purchase quantity. Accordingly, the manufacturer will adjust the retail price of the final product. We assume that adequate supply of new component and production capacity exists. Components carried beyond a price cut can still be used for production after a price cut becomes effective. However, the cost of holding inventory is relatively high due to the price difference. Stock outs are a possible result of a buyer's aversion to holding ending inventory when the price difference is large. Penalty will be incurred in case of stock outs.

For simplicity, we assume that demand associated with stock outs will not be fulfilled after price cuts. We also assume that each final product uses one unit of the component purchased from the supplier. Further, we assume that the price cuts of the electronic component and finished product are synchronized and offered at the same time (Chung et al., 2011). In practice, price cut of the finished product may be offered shortly after the new, lower price of the component becomes effective. However, many electronic retailers offers “price protection” for products purchased prior to a price cut (Lee et al., 2000), which, to a certain extent, synchronizes the price cuts of the component and the finished product.

Consumers may be aware that price of a product will decrease over time. However, they do not know when manufacturers or retailers will reduce the prices. In general, they learn about price reduction of product after the new price has been publicized. As a result, product demand in our model is derived from myopic consumers rather than from strategic buyers (Elmaghraby & Keskinoca, 2003). Delayed purchase due to some consumers’ expectation of new, lower price is assumed insubstantial and is not within the scope of our study.

Finally, we consider a price-sensitive, unit-time market demand D(p), where p is the price of the finished product. D(p) consists of a deterministic term and a stochastic error term (Chung et al., 2011; Ray, Li, & Song, 2005; Zipkin, 2000). The price-sensitive demand functions can be additive or multiplicative. We refer readers Petruzzi and Dada (1999), Ray et al. (2005), and Bernstein and Federgruen (2005) for extensive discussions on the additive and multiplicative demand functions. To simplify the development of the models, we consider the additive demand function; the optimal policies in our models can easily be adapted to multiplicative demand functions.

Besides prices, rapid product innovation and obsolescence will also affect demand. Specifically, the focal component and the finished product go through the typical, four stages of a product life cycle and have relatively short life span (Lee et al., 2000). Although prices of the component and finished product will continue to decline, demand will first increase towards the maturity stage but will start declining even if the prices continue to decrease. Eventually, demand will no longer exist due to the introduction of newer, more versatile components and finished products. As such, price sensitivity will also vary from period to period and price cuts will eventually lose their effectiveness. Typically, untapped market size is small and price sensitivity is low toward the end of a product's life cycle because consumers tend to postpone purchase until the next generation product is introduced. This consumer behavior is evidenced, for example, in smart phones and personal computers. Such demand patterns can be captured by adjusting parameters at
                      and bt
                      in the demand function, both of which are exogenous in our model.

The focal supply chain is fundamentally a decentralized supply chain (DSC) in which each member pursues its own best interest. In contrast, a centralized supply chain (CSC) has one central decision maker that aims at maximizing the system-wide profit. Several supply chain contracts are known to allow a decentralized chain to achieve the centralized profit level (Cachon & Lariviere, 2005). However, price markdown, a widely used discount contract, is not one of them. Therefore, our focus is on deriving the decentralized optimal ordering and pricing policies under the price markdown contract. The centralized profit is used as a benchmark to evaluate the performance of the focal decentralized supply chain.

The notion of double marginalization has been widely observed and discussed in many single-period or two-period models in supply chain contract literature (Dong & Zhu, 2007; Tirole, 1988). It denotes the sequence of mark-ups that leads to a higher retail price and lower combined profit for the supply chain than would be the case if supply chain members were coordinated and integrated. We found that without further contractual agreement, a supply chain characterized by the multiple-period discount mechanism investigated can only achieve around 74–78 percent efficiency. The results seem robust as supply chain efficiencies all fall somewhere in this interval in all experiments we conducted, regardless of the experimental settings. This offers supply chain managers a clear picture about how much improvement a supply chain can achieve if it adopts a coordinating contractual agreement. Potential contracts that may help achieve better supply chain performance in the focal supply chain can be the revenue sharing contract, the buy-back contract, and the two-part tariff contract (Cachon & Lariviere, 2005).

Comparing experiments 1 and 2, we found that when the standard deviation of demand is low (experiment 1), demand, production, price, and profit will be high. The results suggest that higher demand allows supply chain members to raise retail prices and achieve higher level of profit. Interestingly, supply chain efficiencies, however, show mixed results. Specifically, supply chain efficiencies are higher in periods 2 and 3 in experiment 2 when the standard deviations of demand are set at 100 and 150 respectively, as opposed to 50 in experiment 1. Although the decentralized profits of period 2 and 3 are higher in experiment 1 than those in experiment 2, the centralized profits are also higher in experiment 1, resulting in slightly lower supply chain efficiencies. Nonetheless, the impact of demand standard deviation on the optimal policies and supply chain profits and efficiencies are mixed. This can vary with problem setting and parameter values. Our models provide supply chain managers with a tool for analyzing and understanding the effect of demand standard deviation in the multiple-period discount problem investigated.

Among all the factors that may affect supply chain profits, market size parameter in the demand function seems to play the key role. In experiment 1 and 2 we set a fixed market size (i.e., a = 7000), and the profit levels move positively with the optimal production policies, which inherently is decided by the component and product prices. However, when varying the market sizes in experiment 3 to capture the demand patterns across the four stages of product life, the effect of market sizes on the profit levels obviously outweighs the effect of price changes. The main challenge for supply chain managers to adopt our models is, therefore, to identify the set of appropriate parameter values that precisely resembles the demand patterns across the various stages of a product's life cycle. These values are typically selected based on supply chain managers’ knowledge, experience, and judgment about the demand patterns of their products. Historical sales data of previously introduced similar products can be useful in assisting supply chain managers to accurately assess and select parameter values that best depict the demand characteristics. Results presented in experiment 3 are specific to the set of parameter values chosen. Although stylized, its results reasonably capture the characteristics of the life cycle of high-technology products and demonstrate the usefulness of the proposed models. To further understand available techniques developed for describing product life-cycle patterns, we refer readers to Mahajan, Muller, and Bass (1990), Meade and Islam (2006), and Aytac and Wu (2013) for extensive reviews and discussions.

We study a price markdown problem that is commonly observed in the high-technology sector. Electronic components or products typically undergo several price cuts throughout their relatively short life cycles. We develop a multiple-period decision model to address this issue. The main contributions of this research are threefold. First, we develop a decision model that depicts the optimal pricing paths for both a supplier and a buyer when there are several price discounts offered over a product's life span. With such a decision model, suppliers will understand better the potential benefits a component may create in its entire life span, and buyers can make better plans for product development and pricing strategies in different stages of the product's life cycle. Second, extant research in the price discount literature mostly focus on various types of supply chain structures (i.e., 1-1, 1-N, and N-1 supply chains) with single price discount. Studies that investigate multi-period discount pricings, a phenomenon widely seen in the high-tech sector, are still lacking. This paper addresses the need to study multi-period price discount problem. Third, via numerical studies, we demonstrate that the supply chain efficiency of the focal decentralized supply chain is slightly less than 80 percent. This provides supply chain managers with a clear picture as to the room for potential improvement in enhancing supply chain efficiency by adopting coordinated contractual mechanisms.

One of the main restrictions of applying the proposed model is identifying the proper demand function that properly depicts the demand patterns across several stages in a product's life span. The demand function in each period is crucial to correctly identify the optimal pricing and ordering policies. Managers need to carefully assess the parameter values of the demand function in order to obtain valuable insights and better manage the supply chain. Additionally, we only consider the price markdown scheme in this study. An immediate extension is to incorporate other price or quantity discount schemes and identify potential coordinating contractual designs that can achieve higher, better supply chain performance. Furthermore, we investigate the multi-period discount problem associated with a single component and single finished product. In reality, a supplier may offer discounts on multiple components that are adopted by multiple finished products. There is a need to investigate such a scenario in order to understand more thoroughly the multiple period discount problem that is prevalent in the high-technology sector. Finally, we assume that the component and finished product price discounts occur at the same time in this research. However, the discount timing may not be aligned across the entire supply chain. For example, the supplier offers a price markdown on a predetermined day, but the buyer may not take any action to reduce the retail price until later. The model will be more complete if the lack of synchronization of the discount schedules at each echelon of the supply chain is considered.
