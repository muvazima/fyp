@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  This study proposes two location–allocation models for handling uncertainty in the strategic planning of hospital networks. The models aim to inform how the hospital networking system may be (re)organized when the decision maker seeks to improve geographical access while minimizing costs. Key features relevant in the design of hospital networks, such as hospitals being multiservice providers operating within a hierarchical structure, are modelled throughout a planning horizon in which network changes may occur. The models hold different assumptions regarding decisions that have to be taken without full information on uncertain parameters and on the recourse decisions which will be made once uncertainty is disclosed. While the first model is in line with previous literature and considers location as first-stage decisions, the second model considers location and allocation as first-stage decisions. Uncertainty associated with demand is modelled through a set of discrete scenarios that illustrate future possible realizations. Both models are applied to a case study based on the Portuguese National Health Service. The results illustrate the information that can be obtained with each model, how models can assist health care planners, and what are the consequences of different choices on the decisions to be taken without complete information. The second model has shown to be advantageous on grounds that location–allocation decisions are not scenario dependent, and it appears to be more flexible to handle the planning problem at hand.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

Strategic planning of a hospital network involves long term decisions like facility location and capacity relocation, with these decisions needing to be robust so as to face future changes in demand and supply patterns. In particular, decisions related to the location of new hospitals are especially sensitive since they are hardly reversible (due to the high investments involved) and commonly have political impacts. Furthermore, long term decisions need to consider an evolving environment where uncertainty increases as the planning horizon moves forward. On the one hand, the most important decisions are made in the near future, and these decisions need to be made without completely foreseeing the future conditions of the system. On the other hand, decisions made in the distant future can be postponed and revaluated when more information is available (Daskin, Hopp, & Medina, 1992). Therefore, the use of a planning horizon is a model feature not only relevant to assist hospital planning, but also to ensure that decisions account for future prospects and do not restrict upcoming choices.

Additionally, when planning health systems, decision makers need to take into account different policy objectives that typically include improving access and minimizing costs. These objectives can often be conflicting since improved geographical access may require smaller hospital facilities closer to the populations, which can lead to higher inefficiencies and costs. Thus, alternative solutions which balance these two, often conflicting, objectives are relevant in hospital network planning, especially in the context of systems based on a National Health Service (NHS) structure.

Location–allocation models have been used to assist hospital planning, as they allow the simultaneous analysis of decisions as the opening, closing or resizing of facilities, considering the pursuit of multiple objectives. With these models it is also possible to account for the current state of the system and to analyse the impact of future changes in demand and supply patterns, which are deemed as uncertain given that they depend on several external factors (Royston, 2009). In this article, we focus on uncertainty related to the demand and supply of services, more specifically, with the impacts of demand changes from populations that are aging and experiencing increases in life expectancy (typical in developed countries). The work also accounts for the consequences of future changes in hospital supply, given that technological advances are expected to influence the delivery of services and ultimately the projected demand for services.

This article presents two comprehensive stochastic location–allocation models that aim to assist the planning of hospital networks under uncertainty, addressing questions that arise in many real-world applications. The models adopt a stochastic approach and share a general framework, but hold different assumptions regarding the decisions that have to be taken without full information on the uncertain parameters, thus capturing different relevant aspects of hospital network planning. Both models aim to assist the (re)organization of the hospital network, accounting simultaneously for key features in hospital strategic planning, namely: (1) a two-tier hierarchical structure with district and central hospitals, and ascendant and descendant flows between those hierarchical levels; (2) hospitals as multiservice suppliers, providing inpatient, outpatient and emergency services; (3) flows of patients within hospital services; (4) thresholds in hospital capacity; (5) a planning horizon for scheduling decisions throughout time; and (6) a bi-objective approach that deals with tradeoffs between access and cost. Regarding uncertainty, the first model follows the most common assumption in location models where allocation decisions are taken once uncertainty is disclosed. The second model considers that allocation decisions need to be taken before uncertainty is disclosed and consequently unsatisfied demand and extra capacity need to be modelled.

Both models are applied to a case study based in the South Region of Portugal to illustrate how they can assist hospital planning under uncertain conditions. The comparison of the models’ results show the impact of different assumptions regarding allocation decisions, illustrate the effect of demand uncertainty in the (re)organization of the hospital network and indicate the best performing location–allocation decisions for the designed scenarios. Changes in the supply pattern, namely in the average length of stay (ALOS) are analysed through a parameter sensitivity analysis in order to understand the consequences that different parameters have in the hospital network configuration.

This article contributes to health location literature by addressing the hospital network planning under uncertain conditions through two location models that consider several features of many real-world applications. Moreover, the different assumptions regarding the decisions to be taken under uncertainty in the two models are analysed and the nature of the obtained results is compared. The second model, where location–allocation decisions are taken in the first-stage, has shown to be advantageous on grounds of flexibility and by setting the critical decisions for planning not as scenario dependent.

The remainder of this article is organized as follows: Section 2 presents a literature review centred on published general location–allocation models for handling uncertainty and on applications in the health care context; in Section 3, the models’ major assumptions are described and the mathematical formulations presented; Section 4 discusses the models application to the case study; and Section 5 presents some concluding remarks and reflects upon future work.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

Location–allocation models have been used as decision support tools for health planning as described by Daskin and Dean (2004). The literature on location models is extensive: surveys on location studies have been carried out by Owen and Daskin (1998), Marianov and Serra (2004), Klose and Drexl (2005), ReVelle and Eiselt (2005), Smith, Laporte, and Harper (2009b), Melo, Nickel, and Saldanha-da-Gama (2009), Arabani and Farahani (2012), and in the context of health systems by Rahman and Smith (2000).

Location models have been used as tools to support health planning, with different authors focusing on different systems’ aspects. Among others, Narula and Ogbu (1979), Ruth (1981), Moore and ReVelle (1982), Galvão, Espejo, and Boffey (2002), Smith, Harper, Potts, and Thyle (2009a), and Mestre, Oliveira, and Barbosa-Póvoa (2012) have proposed hierarchical models for planning health facilities. Stummer, Doerner, Focke, and Heidenberger (2004), Mitropoulos, Mitropoulos, Giannikos, and Sissouras (2006), and Smith, Harper, and Potts (2012) suggested multiobjective approaches. Santibanez, Bekiou, and Yip (2009) planned clinical services throughout a planning horizon. Recent studies have also focused on re-planning hospitals within network mergers (Gunes & Yaman, 2010) and on the use of a location–allocation model with application to not-for-profit health care organizations (Syam & Côté, 2010). Daskin and Dean (2004) have identified adaptability (defined by the authors as long term uncertainty) and robustness along with reliability as areas with relevance for future applications of location studies in health care. Nevertheless the above mentioned works present deterministic approaches for health care planning. Up to our knowledge, the literature on this topic is still scarce and only the location allocation study by Abdelaziz and Masmoudi (2012) has adopted a stochastic approach for handling uncertainty associated with demand, in the context of assigning beds to hospital departments within a set of existing hospitals. Typically, uncertainty in health systems has been essentially addressed with simulation models or queuing theory (see the comprehensive reviews of simulation in healthcare context by Katsaliaki & Mustafee (2011) and Jahangirian et al. (2012); and simulation models for planning health systems by Harper & Shahani (2002) and Harper, Shahani, Gallagher, & Bowie (2005)). As planning a hospital network involves long-term decisions and future prospects for the system normally entail uncertainty, stochastic approaches are needed to address this problem.

Stochastic approaches based in scenario planning have the advantage of explicitly considering a set of representative scenarios in the mathematical formulation. Thus they identify a solution which performs better under all scenarios, but that may not be optimal for any scenario and therefore not obtainable with scenario analysis. More details on stochastic models can be found in the textbooks by Kall and Wallace (1994) and Birge and Louveaux (1997) and the tutorial by Sen and Higle (1999). Thus, this review focuses on approaches that deal with uncertainty in location–allocation models, emphasizing on aspects that are particularly important for this article.

In the general literature of location models one can find several works and applications of models addressing uncertainty issues, as reviewed by Owen and Daskin (1998) and Snyder (2006). For instance, Schilling (1982) addressed uncertainty as an extension of the maximal covering location problem, by accounting for alternative future scenarios. Louveaux (1986) presented a stochastic version of the capacitated plant location and median problem by considering a two-stage stochastic program with recourse and uncertainty associated with demand, production and transportation. In that study, given that the demands are stochastic and the facilities have minimum capacity requirements, unsatisfied demand was introduced in the model. Later on, Louveaux and Peeters (1992) addressed the computational issues of the plant location problem (in the discrete form) through a heuristic dual-based solution procedure and Laporte, Louveaux, and Vanhamme (1994) presented an optimal algorithm for solving that problem.

Robust approaches for handling uncertainty are frequent in location models and commonly attempt to address worst-case performance or regret (defined as the difference between the objective function value of the solution under the scenario and the optimal solution for that scenario (Daskin & Dean, 2004)). Nevertheless, this criterion is known to be too conservative, since results can be driven by a single extreme (and maybe unlikely) scenario (Owen & Daskin, 1998). Thus, Daskin, Hesse, and Revelle (1997) presented and alternative approach, the α-reliability method, in which worst-case performance is optimized, ensuring that the set of scenarios in use have, at least, α probability of occurrence. This approach appears less conservative and leads to more realistic solutions. Snyder and Daskin (2006) developed the stochastic p-robust location problem that combines two frequent objectives in optimization under uncertainty: the minimization of expected cost and of the worst-case cost or regret. Baron, Milner, and Naseraldin (2011) proposed an alternative robust dynamic model for facility location in a network assuming uncertain demand. The authors followed the approach proposed by Ben-Tal and Nemirovski (1998) in which the uncertainty is confined to a mathematical structure.

Extensions of location models applied in supply chain management and dealing with uncertainty have also been developed by, for instance, Santoso, Ahmed, Goetschalckx, and Shapiro (2005), Tsiakis, Shah, and Pantelides (2001), Georgiadis, Tsiakis, Longinidis, and Sofioglou (2011), and Cardoso, Barbosa-Póvoa, and Relvas (2012); and in the area of reverse logistics, by Listes and Dekker (2005) and Salema, Barbosa-Povoa, and Novais (2007). The stochastic model proposed in this article shares a multiechelon structure with these studies.

Planning hospital networks requires making long-term decisions under uncertain conditions about the future configuration of the system. Nevertheless, extensions of stochastic location models for planning hospital systems considering uncertainty appear to be relatively new, unlike in other research fields such as supply chain management. This work contributes to the health location literature by presenting a general framework to assist the design of hospital networks under uncertain conditions and throughout a planning horizon, while considering key features of hospital networks that arise in many real-world applications. The framework used in this work allows the analysis of the impact of alternative assumptions (regarding the decisions that have to be taken without full information) on uncertain parameters and on the recourse decisions which will be made once uncertainty is disclosed.

This work proposes two models to assist hospital network planning when there is uncertainty associated with demand estimates (note that uncertainty related with the supply of services is later tested with a sensitivity analysis). The models are developed in the context of a planned system based on a NHS structure. NHS key policy goals commonly concern the promotion of equity in access and in the geographic distribution of health resources. Thus, decision makers in NHS based countries have to comprehensively plan the health resources in accordance to the population needs and to pursue these objectives with a limited budget. The models proposed in this work are generic, but when appropriate and/or required, consider the Portuguese NHS as a reference (the Portuguese system is described in detail in Barros, Machado, & Simões (2011)).

In stochastic long-term planning, there are decisions that need to be taken without full information on the uncertainty parameters (first-stage decisions) and others that are made once uncertainty is disclosed (second-stage decisions) also known as corrective actions (Birge & Louveaux, 1997; Kall & Wallace, 1994). In location models, it is common that the location of the facilities is defined by first-stage variables, and the distribution patterns (i.e., in this case allocation decisions) are postponed and defined later in the second-stage (Snyder, 2006). Nevertheless, as pointed out by Birge and Louveaux (1997), the definition of the first-stage vs. second-stage decisions is also context dependent, and there might be advantages in having allocation decisions taken in the first-stage. Accordingly, in the health context, having allocation decisions taken in the first-stage has the advantage of promoting more stable catchment areas, an important feature in public planning. In this work these options regarding the decisions that have to be taken without full information are compared in order to analyse the impact of uncertainty in hospital network (re)organization. Consequently, two location–allocation models are proposed and compared:
                           
                              •
                              A model where location is a first-stage decision and allocation is scenario dependent, i.e., is a recourse action which is taken once uncertainty is disclosed (model described in Section 3.3).

A model with location and allocation as first-stage decisions, i.e., allocation decisions are not scenario dependent, and consequently both location and allocation decisions are taken in the first stage (model described in Section 3.4).

Departing from a study of the location–allocation decision problematique (Bana e Costa, 1996), both models adopt the general framework and share the characteristics illustrated in Fig. 1
                         (with these being further described in Section 3.2). Reading Fig. 1, the models use as inputs information on demand, access and cost measures, on trends in hospital provision and on the existing hospital structure. Since population patterns and utilization rates entail uncertainty (as it will be discussed in detail in Section 3.2.2), demand estimates are captured by a set of discrete scenarios that illustrate future possible outcomes. The trends in hospital provision also entail uncertainty, namely in the average length of stay (ALOS), and as health systems often define and promote targets for ALOS (as policy objectives), variations in these targets will be handled in this work with sensitivity analysis (Section 4.4.1). The models’ outputs illustrate the impact of uncertainty when planning the hospital network under different assumptions for allocation decisions as first vs. second-stage. A hierarchical structure is used along with a planning horizon. For addressing the tradeoffs between improving access and minimizing the cost of (re)organizing and operating the hospital network, a bi-objective programming approach is adopted (Cohon, 1978; Ehrgott, 2005; Eiselt & Sandblom, 2007).

The models consider that hospitals operate in a two-level hierarchy, in which lower level facilities – District Hospitals (DH) – are closer to the populations, provide community-based and non-specialized lower level services to their direct catchment areas; and higher level facilities – Central Hospitals (CH) – provide specialized services to larger catchment areas (receiving/sending patients from/to lower level hospitals). Hospitals are modelled as multiservice providers, delivering inpatient, emergency and outpatient services. Moreover, facilities at different hierarchical tiers cooperate and there are two-directional flows of patients across hospitals in the hierarchy and within hospitals (i.e. between different services in the same hospital).


                           Fig. 2
                            illustrates how the system operates (within and across hospitals) and should be read as follows: given future projections, the population is converted into demand estimates for hospital care using utilization rates. This demand for health services is uncertain (as it will be analysed later in detail), and can be satisfied by a DH or a CH. E.g., if one population is allocated to a lower level facility (DH), it has a probability of being referred to higher level facilities (CH) to receive more specialized services since lower-level hospitals do not provide care of all clinical specialties, and thus patients must be referred to a CH. The reverse flow (from CH to DH) takes place after a treatment is delivered and when the patient no longer needs higher level services and may be monitored in a closer lower level facility. Also, if one patient enters the emergency service, he/she has a probability of being moved to inpatient services within the same facility.

The proposed models are extensions of the constrained p-median model in which the user-facility travel time (or distance) is limited by a standard distance (Holmes, Williams, & Brown, 1972; Toregas, Swain, ReVelle, & Bergman, 1971). Capacity thresholds are imposed in new facilities to ensure quality in the service provided and to justify allocation of public funding (Verter & Lapierre, 2002). These capacity thresholds also limit the scale of hospital production, which prevents hospitals from operating under diseconomies of scale. Candidate locations for new hospitals have a minimum population requirement (Moore & ReVelle, 1982; Rahman & Smith, 1999) ensuring that a chosen location will have both local available resources and a demand to justify a new hospital.

Regarding the allocation criteria, the models are in line with the goal of the Portuguese NHS to fully assign populations to hospitals, by defining compact catchment areas. Consequently, as a planning rule, demand is assigned to the closest open facility (Espejo, Marín, & Rodríguez-Chía, 2012; Gerrard & Church, 1996; Mestre et al., 2012; Teixeira, Antunes, & Peeters, 2007). The use of these constraints is especially relevant when using capacity thresholds, since without them it could result that: demand from the same location would be split between facilities so that minimum capacity requirements to open a new facility are respected; and neighbouring populations could incur in distinct levels of travelling times. These situations are hardly accepted in public facility location (Bigotte & Antunes, 2007; Teixeira & Antunes, 2008; Teixeira et al., 2007), as in that context it can be politically unacceptable that inhabitants from neighbouring areas have different levels of geographic access. Consequently, single and closest assignment constraints should be adopted.

In the proposed models, the uncertainty associated with the demand is captured by a set of discrete scenarios that illustrate future possible realizations of the random parameters. The definition of scenarios is discussed in Section 3.2.2 and detailed in Section 4.1.

As mentioned before, in some contexts it is appropriate to define location decisions as first-stage variables, taken before uncertainty is disclosed, and allocations decisions as second-stage variables. In this study, the first proposed model (Section 3.3) follows this option. Alternatively, it is possible to consider that both location and allocation decisions are first-stage as discussed by Birge and Louveaux (1997). This option is explored in the second model proposed in this study (Section 3.4). In the context of hospital planning, it is expected that the second model provides more stable catchment areas, which is seen as advantageous for public planning because the user-facility assignment does not change according to the demand scenario.

As opposed to deterministic models, when optimizing under uncertainty demand varies in each scenario. The use of discrete scenarios combined with the use of capacity thresholds does not guarantee that all the demand is satisfied in each scenario (Louveaux, 1986). Therefore, in the second model, variables for expected unsatisfied demand and for capacity provided in excess were introduced for capturing the situation where the expected demand is above or below the realized demand. A penalty for both cases is included in the objective function, so as to balance these variables and to avoid undesired extreme situations. Since location–allocation are first-stage decisions, unsatisfied demand and extra capacity are recourse decisions taken once uncertainty is disclosed. One should note that the setting of penalties should be associated with policy judgments, as penalties reflect a trade-off between the model objectives (maximizing access or minimizing costs) and the attainment of another objective: the minimization of unsatisfied demand and of excessive capacity. The setting of penalties is complex and special care should be taken in their choice. Nevertheless, the use of unsatisfied demand/extra capacity in the second model has a significant advantage, since it allows for solutions that may be infeasible for some scenarios, but are expected to perform better when considering the whole set of scenarios. For example, solutions of the second model can hold extreme low/high demand scenarios with buffers for extra capacity/unsatisfied demand.

Capacity decisions are taken in the first-stage and different modelling strategies were adopted since the definition of allocation variables differs in the models. In the first model, allocation decisions are scenario dependent, and thus define both allocation and expected utilization. In this case capacity is higher than or equal to the expected utilization in each scenario, in line with the studies from Birge and Louveaux (1997), Tsiakis et al. (2001), Georgiadis et al. (2011), and Cardoso et al. (2012).

In the second model, capacity is directly related with the allocation decisions since these variables are not scenario dependent. In this case, expected utilization can be calculated using allocation decisions unsatisfied demand and extra capacity.

Both models include a planning horizon that allows scheduling the decisions of (re)organizing the hospital network throughout time and depart from the hospital superstructure that is operating in the beginning of the planning horizon. It is assumed that, throughout the planning horizon, these facilities may be closed, but once closed, they cannot be reopened. On the other hand, new facilities may be opened, and once opened, they cannot be closed (Roodman & Schwarz, 1975, 1977; Van Roy & Erlenkotter, 1982).

Several factors are expected to influence the effective demand and supply of health care delivery. Royston (2009), reporting the UK experience, has identified as main factors affecting the delivery of health care services: lifestyles, public expectations, demography, technology, workforce, and information and communication. In the framework proposed in this article, demand for health services is estimated making use of uncertain information from two datasets that entail uncertainty: population forecasts and utilization rates. Population forecasts predict the evolution of the population pattern and normally are represented in the form of scenarios, based on different assumptions. Analysing the particular case of Portugal, over the last decades an ageing problem has emerged with a significant decrease in the young population and an increase in ageing indicators (INE, 2010). Future scenarios predict a decline in population with reductions in the youngest groups and an increase of the elder populations, also as a consequence of raises in life expectancy and of migratory movements. When analysing demographic projections for Portugal, one finds deviations from the baseline projection due to the underlying demographic assumptions. Therefore, three scenarios can be defined for the population growth in Portugal: pessimistic, baseline and optimistic growth. As shown in Fig. 3
                           , as time moves forward in the planning horizon, these forecasts become more uncertain and tend to diverge from the baseline scenario.

Also, utilization rates influence the demand forecasts. Technological advances in the provision of health care are expected to change the way hospital services are delivered. Accordingly, one can anticipate that the development of outpatient surgery will lead to a decrease in inpatient rates, and technological advances will reduce the average length of stay (ARSNorte, 2009). Additionally, and again taking Portugal as an example, it is undergoing the development of a National Network of Integrated Care (RNCCI) that is expected to decrease the ALOS for acute hospital care (since post-acute treatment or vigilance can be ensured by the RNCCI) and also to reduce the utilization of inpatient services by the elderly population (specially reducing readmissions) (Unidade de Missão para os Cuidados Continuados Integrados, 2009). Nevertheless, it is difficult to anticipate and quantify the impacts of those changes in hospital supply and therefore an approach that deals with uncertainty is crucial.

The model considers two objective functions representing the access and costs objectives. First, the objective function for access in (1) minimizes the expected travel time to reach hospital services weighted by demand. The first element in (1) concerns direct entries in DHs and CHs, respectively, while the second element computes the transfers between facilities in different hierarchical levels (both ascendant and descendent flows in the hierarchy). Secondly, the objective function for costs in (2) minimizes the expected cost, including the operating costs that result from daily activities concerning the use of resources to deliver the services, and the capital costs related to investments with the opening of new facilities and the closure of existing ones.

Demand satisfaction and flow conservation in Eq. (3) ensures that, in each scenario, all demand must be considered – as satisfied by a DH or by a CH. Eqs. (4) and (5) are the flow conservation constraints defined for each scenario that determine the balance between the two tiers of the system hierarchy by defining the two directional flows: Eq. (4) defines the ascendant flow between DH and CH, while Eq. (5) defines the descendent flow between CH and DH, as a share of the ascendant flow.

Eqs. (6)–(11) refer to the hospital capacity. Thus, Eqs. (6) and (7) are auxiliary constraints that assist the estimation of expected utilization in each scenario, respectively for DH and CH. Expected utilization considers the following flows: direct entries in the system, transfers from other services within the same hospital, and patients transferred from other hospitals in a different level of the hierarchy. The flows are converted into expected utilization measured in beds for inpatient service and in episodes for the remaining services. Eqs. (8) and (9) state that, in each scenario, the expected utilization is constrained by hospital capacity (minimum and maximum standards) considering that very small and large hospital operate under diseconomies of scale. Eqs. (10) and (11) determine the value of the capacity to install that cannot be less than the expected utilization in each scenario.

Closest assignment constraints in Eqs. (12)–(16) model the allocation rule with demand points being allocated to the closest opened facility. For example, Eq. (12) determines that if a DH is opened, then the assignment cannot be done to a more distant DH. Eq. (16) refers to the transfers between DH and CH that also need to be made to the closest facility. Eqs. (12)–(16) are adaptations of the Dobson and Karmarkar closest assignment constraints (Espejo et al., 2012; Gerrard & Church, 1996).

Eqs. (17) and (18) ensure that, in each scenario, demand should not take more than a maximum acceptable travel time to access hospital services in a DH and CH, respectively (capturing thus maximum catchment). Eqs. (19) and (20) limit the number of DH/CH opened in each time period and allow for indirectly modelling budget restrictions. Eqs. (21) and (22) assist the choice of potential locations for hospitals candidate sites and establish that a facility may only open if the population is above a minimum standard value.

This formulation provides an adaptation of equations explained in Section 3.3, under the assumption that allocation decisions are represented by first-stage variables. Thus, we abstain from a deeper discussion on the equations that are similar and only highlight the equations that entail major differences. Regarding the objective functions, the model accounts for expected access and cost objectives through the objective functions depicted in Eqs. (32) and (33). However, these constraints now include penalties for unsatisfied demand and extra capacity that are needed because demand is varying in each scenario and there are minimum capacity requirements. Eq. (34) now states that all demand is considered by the model, either as satisfied or unsatisfied demand.

Eqs. (48)–(50) are valid inequalities which can be used in the model to improve the computational performance. These constraints limit the flows between demand and hospital facility, namely: Eqs. (48) and (49) ensure that any demand point can only be served by an opened facility, while Eq. (50) guarantees that transfers are only made to opened facilities.

It should be noted that we assume in both models that investment costs associated with building, expanding and closing hospitals are fixed, not varying with hospital size.

To illustrate the usefulness of the proposed models, we apply them to a case study based on the South Region of the Portuguese NHS. The results aim to show the impact of data uncertainty when using a stochastic approach and how the models can be used to assist hospital network planners in analysing location and allocation decisions on that context, rather than providing a unique optimal answer for the reorganization of hospitals. As far as the provision of health care services is concerned, the South region of Portugal is an independent and self-sufficient area that comprises three Administrative Health Regions: Lisbon and Tagus Valley, Alentejo and Algarve. It has a population of approximately 4 million inhabitants, is divided in 109 smaller area population units (which correspond to the unit used for analysis), and integrates areas with different characteristics: Lisbon is an highly populated urban region with good physical accessibilities and higher proximity to health services; Alentejo is a rural and remote area with poor accessibility to services and with a decreasing and ageing population; and the Algarve is a touristic area experiencing high variations in its population due to the seasonal nature of tourism activities. In the current network, CHs are located in the Lisbon metropolitan area, while DHs are spread across the Southern region. The reorganization of the current network of hospitals in Portugal has been under public discussion, with several projects under analysis, including: the reorganization of the capacity in the Lisbon metropolitan area with the opening of a new facility in Loures; and projects for the construction of CHs in Alentejo (Évora) and the Algarve (between Faro-Loulé). The results presented in this section depart from the existing superstructure and analyse which may be best reorganization alternatives to improve access and/or costs.

This section proceeds with a description of the models’ parameters (defined in Section 4.1) and with illustrative results from running the proposed models with the ε-constraint method of multiobjective programming (Cohon, 1978; Ehrgott, 2005; Eiselt & Sandblom, 2007), so as to deal with the tradeoffs between access and cost. Results were obtained using the General Algebraic Modelling System (GAMS) with CPLEX 12 in a Intel(R) Core™ i3 CPU M350@2.27GHz.

This section describes the information from the case study used to run the models. The population in need of inpatient services was estimated using historical data from the diagnostic-related group (DRG) database system which contains records for all the patients entering Portuguese NHS hospitals for inpatient care. Utilization rates reflect the demographic (and sex) structure of populations. Following the discussion in Section 3.2.2, uncertainty in demand estimates can be caused by alternative assumptions regarding population forecasts and utilization rates. Two features need to be accounted for when setting utilization rates:
                           
                              •
                              The overuse of emergency services in Portugal can be reduced by reinforcements of programmed activity in external consultations, which provide patients with more preventive and adequate services. As a result, the following rates are projected for 2020: 460 episodes per 1000 inhabitants for emergency service and 1500 appointments per 1000 inhabitants for outpatient service – these are targets set by (ARSNorte, 2009).

Utilization rates for inpatient services are expected to decrease due to the evolution of outpatient surgery and the development of a National Network of Integrated Care (RNCCI), which will extend the provision of long term care. An earlier study has identified future demand scenarios for inpatient services considering the development of outpatient surgery according to two scenarios: a more conservative one that would lead to a decrease of 6.7% in the demand for inpatient services, and a less conservative scenario of a 12.3% decrease (ARSNorte, 2009). The impact of the RNCCI is difficult to predict since it is still a recent and ongoing project.

Accordingly, future utilization entails uncertainty and will be represented by scenarios, described in detail in 4.2. Technological advances and the RNCCI are also expected to reduce the average length of stay (ALOS) of inpatient services. Although current values are approximately 7.4 for DH and 8 days for CH, the target has been set to 6 days for both DH and CH. These figures will be used for sensitivity analyses.

The second model proposed in this work uses penalties since unsatisfied demand and extra capacity were introduced. The purpose of these variables is to allow solutions that may be infeasible for a particular scenario but that are expected to perform better when considering the whole set of scenarios. These variables represent buffers for demand/capacity and thus a stochastic solution may hold extreme low/high demand scenarios that otherwise were not possible. Although, as mentioned above, the choice of penalties is complex and may be associated with policy judgements, in this case study we have used penalties that avoid extreme and inefficient situations of high levels of unsatisfied demand and of excessive capacity. Accordingly, we have run the model for several penalty values and chose the penalty levels that allowed a balanced situation and did not create high extra capacity which is not due to uncertainty neither unsatisfied demand – these values are presented in the last row of Table 3

We now present the results obtained with the model where location and allocation are first-stage decisions, and capacity thresholds are imposed to the expected capacity, including a buffer for unsatisfied demand and extra capacity. The strategy used in this section is slightly different from the previous sections. In this case, the changes in the ALOS parameter are also considered through a sensitivity analysis, and consequently different Pareto curves are defined providing more detailed information on the impact of this parameter on the results. These results are discussed based on the expected values of the ALOS parameter, since as referred above, it was considered more relevant to understand the consequences that different targets for ALOS have in the hospital network structure, more specifically in the expected hospital capacity. A careful analysis of this aspect is more relevant in this second model since location and allocation decisions are both taken in the first-stage which, despite the above mentioned advantages in terms of public planning, may raise extra concerns due to the need of results being robust to face future uncertainty and due to the fact that the recursive/corrective actions in the second model (i.e. unsatisfied demand and extra capacity) have less influence in the hospital network structure than in the first model (allocation decisions). Consequently the results of the model are shown for simultaneous consideration of three designed scenarios (s1′, s2′ and s3′), as reported in Table 6
                        . Section 4.4.2 illustrates in detail the nature of the model results using alternative scenarios generated with a triangular distribution, which was found to be suitable for the available data that defined the baseline, the pessimist and the optimist values.

This section reports the stochastic results obtained with the application of the model using the three scenarios described in Table 6.

The results from the deterministic model in high and low-demand scenario previously described (Section 4.2) provide relevant information for this stochastic model. The low-demand scenario provides improved valid lower bounds for hospital capacity of initial existing facilities, while the high demand scenario (without the opening of new facilities) provides valid upper bounds. The Pareto frontiers for the stochastic model considering simultaneously the three scenarios (s1′, s2′ and s3′) under different assumptions for ALOS are presented in Fig. 6

Results in Fig. 6 suggest that the location of new hospitals is robust to variations in ALOS: despite points A, A′ and A″ correspond to different combinations of cost and access when access is maximized, they suggest the construction of new hospitals in the same locations, independently of the ALOS; the same occurs to points B, B′ and B′, in which costs are minimized, as well as for points C, C′ and C″, and for D, D′ and D″. Nonetheless, despite decreases in ALOS do not impact on locations they have a high impact on total costs.

Comparing the results of both models, one observes that solutions obtained with model 2 are not constrained by a single extreme scenario as occurred in model 1. The introduction of variables for unsatisfied demand and extra capacity in model 2 provide flexibility to accommodate extreme scenarios without constraining the results. Additionally, in model 2, location–allocation decisions are taken in the first-stage, with the model providing more suitable information for public planning (as will be further illustrated in Section 4.4.2). In this model allocation decisions do not depend on the scenario, and consequently it is possible to define stable catchment areas and properly inform the users. Also model 2 provides single values for recommended capacity, while model 1 provides an interval between low and high demand scenarios. For these reasons we consider that model 2 presents a more suitable approach for handling demand uncertainty in the context of hospital network planning, and for this model we compute the Expected Value of Perfect Information (EVPI) that measures the value of having complete information about the future (Birge & Louveaux, 1997) regarding the whole set of uncertain parameters – see Fig. 6. The EVPI compares the stochastic solution (also known as here-and-know or recourse problem) with the Expected Value of the optimal solution (or wait-and-see solution) that is obtained when the stakeholder has complete information on the random variables and thus makes the proper choice obtaining maximum benefit (Madansky, 1960). This value is computed solving a deterministic model corresponding to each scenario. In the context of the bi-objective model, in Point A – improved expected access –, the EVPI indicator captures losses in accessibility due to the presence of uncertainty, while in Point B – minimum expected cost –, it represents the expected benefit of perfect foresight. The high values obtained for the EVPI indicator show the importance of the stochastic solution and consequently the relevance of accounting for uncertainty when planning networks of hospitals.

The three scenarios previously designed capture extreme situations (high and low demand scenarios) and an intermediate scenario. Although possible, these extreme scenarios are unlikely to occur. Therefore, it is necessary to model intermediate scenarios that, in this section, will be generated using the triangular distribution in the absence of more detailed empirical data. The triangular distribution was chosen because it is found appropriate when data is asymmetric (Morgan & Henrion, 1990), as it is the case of our dataset. The scenarios s1′, s2′ and s3′ were used to generate further scenarios which will be used in the model to describe future possible realizations of demand. Accordingly, the intermediate scenario (s2′) was used as the most likely value and s1′ and s3′ as the lower and upper limit values. The results were obtained by generating one hundred scenarios that in our experimentation have proved to provide accurate results. To determine this number, a computational study was carried out to test the effect of increasing the number of scenarios and we observed that beyond one hundred scenarios the model outcomes are not sensitive to the increase in the number of scenarios. This result was confirmed using statistical inference as proposed by You, Wassick, and Grossmann (2009). In order to illustrate the effect of raising the number of scenarios on computational performance, Table 7
                            contains some model statistics, including the number of variables and equations, and the computational time.

Points C–G represent alternative solutions for the tradeoff between expected access and cost that could be considered by the decision maker. In this study, the planning of hospital networks under uncertainty was addressed using a set of discrete scenarios that capture future possible realizations of the system. Two mathematical models were developed as to capture different assumptions regarding the decisions that have to be taken when full information on the uncertain parameters is not available. Despite representing different approaches for addressing uncertainty, the models share the same comprehensive framework by considering issues that may arise in real-world applications of hospitals operating in a hierarchical network and providing multiple services. The tradeoffs between improved access and minimum cost are addressed using the ε-constrained method of multiobjective programming, providing the decision maker with alternative solutions for planning. A planning horizon is included in the models allowing for the decisions of (re)organizing the hospital network to be scheduled throughout time. The models were designed under the context of a health care system based on a NHS structure, and a case study based on the Portuguese NHS was used to test the applicability of the models.

This work contributes to the health location literature by addressing hospital planning in the presence of uncertainty associated with the demand and supply of hospital services and considering several features relevant in many real-world applications. The application of the models to the case study illustrates the nature of the results which can be obtained with each model.

The model with location as first-stage decisions follows the most common assumption in the literature of location models where allocation decisions are scenario dependent, i.e., recourse actions that will be taken once uncertainty is disclosed. The application of the model to the case study has shown that, in the presence of capacity thresholds, the solutions can be driven by a single extreme scenario with low demand, which can be seen as too restrictive for planning under uncertainty.

Alternatively a model considering location and allocation as first-stage decisions was developed. In this model location–allocation decisions are not considered to be scenario dependent, and since demand is varying in each scenario and the model has capacity thresholds, variables for unsatisfied demand and extra capacity were introduced. This model has shown to be more flexible for the context of hospital network planning within the framework in use.

The models presented in this article consider hospitals as multiservice providers. Yet, when reporting to the case study, only three services were considered and the demand was aggregated. This option was made due to limited information regarding utilization of services and supply at the clinical specialties level. Nevertheless, we consider that it may be worth, using the outputs of the models proposed in this article, to explore the use of a second phase model or of a simulation model to distribute beds across clinical specialties. Alternatively, it may be relevant to adapt the proposed models so that they define networks of providers (and referral networks) at the clinical specialty level, following research developed by Stummer et al. (2004) at the medical department level.

This study has focused on uncertainty related to the demand for hospital services, since previous studies have shown that demand has a major influence in location–allocation decisions. Nevertheless, other parameters might also entail uncertainty and justify further analysis, for instance: transfer rates, travel time for patients to reach a health facility and costs for health care delivery. The model includes a planning horizon attempting to capture the evolutionary aspect of the (re)organization process. As referred, the most important decisions are made in the near future, since long term decisions may be revaluated when more information is disclosed. This process may be implemented in the proposed model by a rolling horizon, where past decisions are fixed. Nevertheless, a multi-stage model may better capture the dynamics of the planning process (as presented by Nickel, Saldanha-da-Gama, & Ziegler (2012) in the context of supply chain management), and thus it should be considered for future research. It would also be relevant to develop the model outputs for better decision support.
