@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  We analyze the problem of technology selection and capacity investment for electricity generation in a competitive environment under uncertainty. Adopting a Nash-Cournot competition model, we consider the marginal cost as the uncertain parameter, although the results can be easily generalized to other sources of uncertainty such as a load curve. In the model, firms make three different decisions: (i) the portfolio of technologies, (ii) each technology’s capacity and (iii) the technology’s production level for every scenario. The decisions related to the portfolio and capacity are ex-ante and the production level is ex-post to the realization of uncertainty. We discuss open and closed-loop models, with the aim to understand the relationship between different technologies’ cost structures and the portfolio of generation technologies adopted by firms in equilibrium. For a competitive setting, to the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first not only to explicitly discuss the relation between costs and generation portfolio but also to allow firms to choose a portfolio of technologies. We show that portfolio diversification arises even with risk-neutral firms and technologies with different cost expectations. We also investigate conditions on the probability and cost under which different equilibria of the game arise.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

The restructuring of the electricity sector into a competitive market requires changes in generators’ investment policies. In a non-competitive environment in which companies had fewer interactions with respect to technology portfolio and capacity decisions, traditional optimization techniques were enough to address typical power investment problems. However, in a competitive setting, one company’s decision has a significant impact on its competitors; for instance a capacity expansion decision can directly affect market prices. Consequently, game theoretical techniques in which strategic interaction between agents are taken into consideration are more suited to analyze generation investment policies for restructured electricity markets. Moreover, perfect competition may not be the most appropriate way to model the power sector given market concentration. An oligopolistic environment in which firms can exercise some market power is more suitable. Thus, the design of electricity generation investment policies needs to accommodate the characteristics of this restructured environment.

However, competition is just one issue which impacts the complexity of electricity investments. Cost uncertainty is a major component of investment problems and many factors can contribute to that. The uncertain status of greenhouse gases emission abatement programs is certainly one of them. These programs have been discussed around the world and have uncertain global implementation. Among the mechanisms available, emissions tax and cap-and-trade are the most common. Independent of which mechanism is implemented, these programs have significant impact on marginal production costs. The high volatility of fuel prices is another major cost factor, as evidenced by recent natural gas prices. Furthermore, the different demands that firms face during different seasons of the year, days of the week and even periods of the day are inherent features of electricity markets. Thus, it is difficult to argue for a model of investment in electricity markets without considering cost and demand uncertainty.

Recently, Banal-Estañol and Micola (2009) investigated the relationship between diversification in electricity portfolios and electricity prices. If we assume that there is some relation between the portfolio of generation technologies and market prices, then it becomes worthwhile to explore the relation between technologies’ cost and the generation portfolio in equilibrium. Power generation corporations, in general, have multiple technologies in their portfolios; for instance coal, gas, nuclear and others. Thus, there is no reason to constrain the firm’s choice to one or the other technology when electricity generation investment policies are evaluated. An electricity competition model should allow firms to have more than one technology in their portfolio.

Considering these features of the electricity sector, we address technology and capacity investment problems faced by electricity generators in a competitive environment and under uncertainty. We allow firms to exercise market power in an oligopoly à la Cournot. Our results are general to problems with uncertain marginal costs and different inverted demand curves (load curve), but we focus the discussion on marginal cost uncertainties. Our aim is to understand the relationship between different technologies’ cost structures and the generation portfolio of technologies adopted by firms in equilibrium. For a competitive setting, to the best of our knowledge, our paper is the first not only to explicitly discuss the relation between costs and generation portfolio but also to allow firms to choose a portfolio of technologies. We assume that firms have similar efficiency levels, then they will also experience similar investment and marginal costs for the same technology.

In our model, firms make three different decisions: (i) the portfolio of technologies, (ii) each technology’s capacity and (iii) each technology’s production level for every scenario. The decisions related to the portfolio and capacity are ex-ante and the production level is ex-post to the realization of uncertainty. We consider the marginal cost as the uncertain parameter, although our results can be generalized easily to other sources of uncertainty such as a load curve. We start our discussion using a stochastic open-loop game in which firms adapt to the realization of uncertain parameters, also called S-adapted open-loop strategies by Haurie, Zaccour, and Smeers (1990). Despite being half way between a deterministic open-loop game and a dynamic closed-loop, this stochastic open-loop game

                      with these three decisions has the mathematical structure of a one stage game. However, we overcome this limitation by providing a closed-loop model. In this case, we focus on the symmetric subgame perfect equilibrium (SSPE). The equilibrium assumes symmetric levels of investment, but provides a recourse in case firms deviate from this strategy. The closed-loop model is a dynamic game in which, in the first stage, firms decide on the portfolio of technologies and their capacities and, in the second stage, after the uncertainty is revealed, they decide the production in a Cournot game. In this case, we have an equilibrium problem in the first stage (portfolio and capacity) subject to equilibrium constraints in the second stage (production).

Open-loop and closed-loop games have different information structures, as presented in Fig. 1
                     . In closed-loop games all the firms’ past plays are known in the beginning of each stage. As a consequence, a firm will have to consider the best response not only to equilibrium actions of prior stages, but also to any potential deviations. In contrast, in open-loop games firms do not observe the opponents’ play considering the opponents do not deviate from equilibrium (Fudenberg & Levine, 1988). This difference in information structure makes open-loop much more tractable than closed-loop games (Basar & Olsder, 1999; Fudenberg & Levine, 1988; Fudenberg & Tirole, 1991). In practice, the open-loop model would be more appropriate for an electricity market organized with Power Purchase Agreement, while the closed-loop model is more applicable to a spot market structure (Murphy & Smeers, 2005). During the paper, we connect the open-loop and closed-loop models by showing that the SSPE is also the equilibrium for the open-loop game.

Our main result is that portfolio diversification arises even with risk-neutral firms and technologies with different cost expectations. Furthermore, we characterize the production level of each firm for each possible scenario realization, in other words each combination of cost and demand realization. Conditions that yield to diversification (or not) are discussed in the paper. For the open-loop model, under our assumptions, in aggregate terms firms always invest and produce the same quantities. Asymmetry on the use of technologies between firms can occur, but it only happens when technologies have the same cost expectation.

The paper is organized as follows. In the next section, we provide a literature review which is followed by a background on the model. Section 4 is an open-loop model in which we discuss properties of the problem and a duopoly with two scenarios. In Section 5, we present an analysis of the case with an arbitrary number of firms and scenarios based on a closed-loop model. The relationship of the open vs. closed-loop models and a discussion about socially optimal investment are respectively in Sections 6.1 and 6.2. Finally, we conclude the paper with also some future research directions. To enable an easier reading, we do not provide any mathematical proof in the main text. All the proofs are in the paper’s E-Appendix.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

Game theory has received considerable attention in electricity markets (Kahn, 1998; Kleindorfer, Wu, & Fernando, 2001; Ventosa, Baíllo, Ramos, & River, 2005), and, it has become increasingly relevant to electricity investment decisions. We separated the literature on electricity investment in a competitive environment into two parts: one relying more on computational results and the other on highly stylized models whose purpose is to understand the strategic interaction of firms in markets that are highly concentrated. We also notice that, in one stream of highly stylized models, different firms use just one technology (symmetric firms) and, in another stream, different firms use different technologies but are constrained to use just one of them (asymmetric firms).

We start with computationally oriented models. Chuang, Wu, and Varaiya (2001) present a Cournot model for generation expansion, incorporating operational considerations such as capacity limitations and energy balance. Firms decide only on the type of technologies and capacity level for investment, since it is assumed that firms fully bid their total capacity at marginal cost. Linares, Santos, Ventosa, and Lapiedra (2006) provide a detailed model with investment costs and emissions trading for the Spanish market, with investment decisions being characterized as a Nash-Cournot equilibria. Centeno, Reneses, García, and Sánchez (2003) formulate some algorithms on electricity capacity expansion with firms competing in quantities, and they point out the difficulties of accounting for uncertainty in these large scale problems. Under demand uncertainty, Pineau and Murto (2003) apply the concept of S-adapted open-loop strategies to an investment game with Cournot firms for the Finnish electricity market which is also revisited by Pineau, Rasata, and Zaccour (2011). Similarly, Genc, Reynolds, and Sen (2007) formulate some stochastic programming problems for S-adapted open-loop games. This model is tested in the Ontario electricity market in Genc and Sen (2008). Sauma and Oren (2006) provide a game that integrates the network planner transmission expansion projects with generators’ capacity decision. An agent-based approach in which Cournot firms adapt (trade) their generation portfolios is presented by Bunn and Oliveira (2007). Ehrenmann and Smeers (2011) study a capacity expansion game with stochastic demand and costs, but considering the firms as price takers. They discuss the formulation and numerical results for risk-neutral and risk-averse firms. Computational models providing Markov Perfect Equilibrium results for investment decisions are presented by Murto, NSsSkkSlS, and Keppo (2004) and Bushnell and Ishii (2007). They mention the difficulties of showing existence and uniqueness for this type of model.

For highly stylized models with symmetric firms, Gabszewicz and Poddar (1997) present a two stage investment model with uncertain demand. The authors focus on finding the symmetric subgame perfect equilibrium (SSPE) for the problem, and on the impact that demand uncertainty has on capacity utilization. Garcia and Shen (2010) study a very stylized model for which they provide analytical solutions for a multi-period closed-loop investment game with stochastic demand growth and Cournot firms. Their aim is to understand the equilibrium level of investment adopted by firms when they exercise market power in the short-run. Also focusing on Markov Perfect Equilibrium, a dynamic model to explore some behavioral aspects of investments in electricity markets is discussed by Garcia and Stacchetti (2009). Different market configurations are used in Boom and Buehler (2007–2009) to understand the impact of restructuring electricity markets in capacity investments. Price caps (Zoettl, 2008) and forward markets (Grimm & Zoettl, 2006) are other topics in the context of one technology and capacity investment with symmetric firms.

We next summarize highly stylized models with firms investing in different and dedicated technologies. Murphy and Smeers (2005) provide three models for generation capacity expansion: one for perfect competition, and open-loop and closed-loop models in which firms can exercise market power in a Cournot setting. This study is also extended to the presence of forward contracts in Murphy and Smeers (2010). Oliveira (2008) present a game very similar to the open-loop model of Murphy and Smeers (2005), but the author evaluates how perfect rationality influences these investment models. Garcia, Alzate, and Barrera (2012) discuss in a two stage game the impact of feed-in tariffs and mandatory portfolio standards on investment in a conventional and a renewable technology. In the second stage firms compete in prices.

Our study is a Nash-Cournot competition model as are the majority of the works presented in this literature review.

First, we can discuss some properties that are general to the open-loop model without the two scenarios assumption. Asymmetry between firms and non-uniqueness only arise from cases in which technologies are equivalent (indifference case). The asymmetry result does not guarantee diversification, because firms could still invest in just one technology. Non-uniqueness and asymmetry are closely related in this investment problem. They always happen together and can only occur when technologies have the same cost expectation. Assuming that, in general, technologies are not equivalent, then this model, even with linear costs, presents a unique equilibrium and it is symmetric between firms.

The duopoly model presented in the last section gives some insights on the problem, but one might argue that it is a simplification which limits interpretation for the electricity sector. One inherent feature of electricity markets is the different load curves. Furthermore, the high volatility of fuel costs, often neglected in investment models in electricity, and different policies that governments can impose on generators make a model with just two scenarios for cost uncertainty very improbable. Thus, a more realistic model should accommodate uncertainty on marginal costs and demand together without a limitation on the number of scenarios. Furthermore, the stochastic open-loop game has the mathematical structure of a one stage game. To have a two stage game, we would need to define the subgame perfect equilibrium. We implement these new characteristics for the model in the next section, which also considers an arbitrary number of firms.

In this section, we develop a model for an arbitrary number of firms and scenarios, which is more appropriate for electricity markets. Thus, it accounts for multiple scenarios with cost and demand uncertainty, but we keep the discussion focused on cost uncertainty. This model is a closed-loop formulation with a subgame perfect equilibrium. We show some results for an asymmetric subgame perfect equilibrium, which are then used to construct a symmetric subgame perfect equilibrium (SSPE) with an approach similar to Gabszewicz and Poddar (1997). This closed-loop game has two stages. First, firms decide on the portfolio of technologies and capacities. In the second stage, firms define their production level for each technology in a Cournot game.

To understand the properties of the investment decision, we address the problem recursively. First, in Section 5.1, we present the results for the second stage of the game: the production decision. For the second stage, we separate the discussion on the asymmetric equilibrium (Section 5.1.1) and, then, on the symmetric equilibrium for the production (Section 5.1.2). In Section 5.2, with the results from the second stage, we analyze the SSPE for the first stage: portfolio of technologies and capacities decisions. The analysis of the first stage gives the complete results for this investment game (Section 5.2.1). In Section 5.2.2, after developing the necessary results, we discuss some of the properties of the investment decision which include conditions for diversification. In Fig. 3

This result completes the analysis of the closed-loop model with multiple scenarios which fulfills the need for an electricity model with cost and demand uncertainty.

In this section, we focus first on the relationship between the open and the closed-loop models. We also briefly address the type of the electricity market structure for which each model would be appropriate. In Section 6.2, we evaluate if the investment outcome is socially optimal.

We have already discussed how the information structure of the open and closed-loop equilibrium differ. As discussed by Fudenberg and Levine (1988), in the open-loop game the opponent’s past plays are not observed, while in the closed-loop the past decisions are known in the beginning of each stage. Based in Sections 4 and 5, we could notice that the open-loop model was much more tractable than the closed-loop model. As studied by Haurie et al. (1990), Haurie and Moresino (2002) and Genc et al. (2007), our open-loop equilibrium is also adapted for random parameters.

Each of these two models is appropriate for a different type of market structure. On one hand, in the open-loop model, the portfolio, capacity and production decisions are decided simultaneously within a one stage game. As discussed by Murphy and Smeers (2005), it is a Power Purchase Agreement market in which capacity of each technology is simultaneously built and sold on long-term contracts. This became quite common in Brazil in the last decade. On the other hand, in the closed-loop model, the portfolio and capacity decisions are made in the first stage and the production in the second stage. This is similar to an environment in which firms build their capacities and compete in the spot market.

                           The issue of open and closed loop models in electricity markets is discussed with more details in Murphy and Smeers (2005).

@&#CONCLUSION@&#

The electricity market has been transitioning to a competitive environment around the world. The direct impact of electricity generation investment decisions on market prices makes it important to capture the interaction between generating firms when investing. However, competition is just one of the complexities within this industry. There is a lot of uncertainty on marginal costs caused by implementation of government policies and volatility on fuel costs. The uncertain demand for electricity during different periods is another issue. Furthermore, many generating firms present a portfolio of production technologies. Thus, a good investment model should allow firms to invest and compete with more than one technology. We addressed those issues in an investment model for electricity generators.

In this study, we focused on the selection of technologies and capacity investment for electricity generation in a competitive environment. We considered uncertainty on the marginal cost which can also be reinterpreted for joint uncertainty on costs and demand. We also allowed firms to choose from a portfolio of technologies. We started by presenting an open-loop model for which we discussed properties and exemplified it with a duopoly with two scenarios. Then, we analyzed the SSPE for a model for n scenarios. The SSPE was obtained in a closed-loop model in which portfolio and capacity was decided in the first stage and production level in the second. We connected the open-loop and closed-loop models by showing that the SSPE was also the equilibrium for the open-loop game. Furthermore, we showed that investment outcome is not socially optimal.

Our aim was to understand the relationship between different technologies’ cost structures and the generation portfolio of technologies adopted by firms in equilibrium. We generalized the results of Gabszewicz and Poddar (1997) to two technologies, and, because of the portfolio decision, we partially capture the interaction of different technologies presented in Murphy and Smeers (2005). Our main result was that portfolio diversification arises even with risk-neutral firms and technologies with different cost expectations. We also characterized the production level of each firm for each possible scenario realization. We provided conditions that yield (or not) to diversification. For the open-loop model, in aggregate terms, firms always invest and produce the same quantities. Asymmetry on the use of technologies between firms can occur, but it only happens when technologies have the same cost expectation.

Our paper presents some limitations. We believe that entry costs should be explored, as it can cause changes in the dynamics of the problem, and the equilibrium structure (Vives, 1999). Transmissions should also be considered together with the portfolio choice in a future study. The issue of risk-averse firms is another opportunity. Ehrenmann and Smeers (2011) consider stochastic demand and stochastic costs together with risk-averse firms, but it would be interesting to further explore more the properties of this problem. The inclusion of an initial portfolio for the firms could be also an extension. Moreover, the addition of other parameters and decisions would enable the discussion of issues such as operational hedging and backup investment which were studied in another environment by Sting and Huchzermeier (2010).
