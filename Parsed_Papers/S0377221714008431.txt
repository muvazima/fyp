@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  A bullwhip measure for a two-stage supply chain with an order-up-to inventory policy is derived for a general, stationary SARMA(p, q) × (P, Q)
                        s
                      demand process. Explicit expressions for several SARMA models are obtained to illustrate the key relationship between lead-time and seasonal lag. It is found that the bullwhip effect can be reduced considerably by shortening the lead-time in relation to the seasonal lag value.
               
            

@&#INTRODUCTION@&#

When retailers submit orders to their suppliers in response to demand, they must carefully balance inventory levels and expected future demand. Ensuring that the inventory can meet consumer demand often results in higher fluctuations in the ordering process than the variability of demand would initially suggest. This phenomenon is called the bullwhip effect and can depend on many factors such as the ordering policy, the lead-time for an order to arrive, and even the demand process itself. Lee et al. (1997a); 1997b) have illustrated the existence of this effect at firms such as Proctor & Gamble Co. and Hewlett-Packard Company. The distortion of information as orders go upstream in a supply chain has been studied from a mathematical perspective by examining the magnitude of the bullwhip effect under various settings. In this paper, we consider a single-item, two-stage supply chain with an order-up-to inventory policy and derive a bullwhip effect measure for a general class of demand models: seasonal autoregressive and moving average processes (i.e., SARMA(p, q) × (P, Q)
                        s
                     ). We use this theory to show the crucial links among the magnitude of the bullwhip effect, the lead-time, and seasonal lag.

There is a large body of work showing that demand has seasonal components. Wray (1958, pp. 4) writes, for example, that toy purchases are seasonal with demand rising around the Christmas holidays. Baltas (2005) and Heien (1983) examine the seasonal behavior of the demand for nondurable goods such as laundry detergent, food, and energy. Board (2008) and Heien (1983) also examine housing and Board (2008) continues his analysis for other durable goods. Finally, for a more general discussion examining cyclical demand across industries and countries, see Beaulieu et al. (1992).

Furthermore, seasonal demand has been previously considered within the operations and supply chains context – just not, as far as we know, in the bullwhip literature. For example Crowston et al. (1973) and McClain and Thomas (1977) focus on production planning for goods which are in demand on a seasonal basis. Sethi and Cheng (1997) and Aviv and Federgruen (2001) study inventory systems for products with seasonal demand as well. Finally, Bradley and Arntzen (1999) combine production planning and inventory policy along with capacity in the presence of seasonal demand. Given this large body of literature and that the demand process is a key driver of the bullwhip effect, we feel it is critical to incorporate demand seasonality in our measurements.

This paper is organized as follows. We begin with a review of the existing bullwhip effect literature in Section 2. In Section 3, we outline the supply chain framework for one supplier and one retailer where the latter employs an order-up-to inventory policy and mathematically define the bullwhip measure. In Section 4, we describe the SARMA(p, q) × (P, Q)
                        s
                      model. Sections 3 and 4 provide the foundation for the main result. Next, in Section 5, we derive the bullwhip measure for the general SARMA demand case. We apply our theorem to the AR(1), MA(q), and ARMA(1,1) settings to connect our new results to previous work. In Section 6, we consider the SARMA(1, 0)
                        s
                     , SARMA(0, 1) × (1, 0)
                        s
                     , and SARMA(1, 0) × (0, 1)
                        s
                      cases, deriving formulas analytically from the main theorem. We examine the conditions under which the bullwhip effect exist focusing specifically on the key relationship between the seasonal lag and the lead-time between placing and receiving an order. Moreover, we show how to reduce the bullwhip effect by exploiting this relationship. Finally, we provide some concluding remarks in Section 7.

@&#BACKGROUND@&#

For a general overview of the bullwhip effect and its causes, see Lee et al. (1997a). Luong (2007),Luong and Phien (2007), and Duc et al. (2008) derive explicit expressions for the bullwhip measure for the AR(1), AR(p) and ARMA(1,1) demand processes respectively. We will be generalizing their results in this paper. Graves (1999) has examined the ARIMA(0,1,1) case as well. Gilbert (2005) and Galmaan (2006) examine the bullwhip effect on orders and inventory for ARMA and ARIMA models. Gilbert (2005) considers a slightly different setup than what we do here. Ma and Wu (2014) look at AR(1) and simple ARMA models for multi-stage supply chains. However, the effects of seasonality are not considered.

A second approach focuses on comparing the magnitude of the bullwhip effect among different forecasting methods. For example, Chen et al. (2000) forecast demand with exponential smoothing. Zhang (2004b) compares three forecasting methods: minimizing the mean-squared forecasting error (used by many researchers including us), moving average, and exponential smoothing. He finds that the bullwhip measure can vary based on the forecasting method applied. Hayya et al. (2006) examine forecasting methods with an additional focus on the impact of lead-times in the bullwhip analysis. Marchena (2011) examines estimation methods for ARMA demand models.

A final stream of research concentrates on ways to reduce the bullwhip effect. Alwan et al. (2003) study demand forecasting methods which can reduce the bullwhip effect specifically for AR(1) and ARMA(1,1) demand processes. Wang, Jia, and Takahashi (2005) investigate how unforeseen events, such as demand shocks, influence their extended bullwhip effect measure. They also discuss how information sharing between the retailer and supplier can help eliminate the bullwhip effect altogether. Gill and Abend (1997) show this in action through their case study on Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. Chatfield et al. (2004) investigate the bullwhip effect especially in the case of variable lead-times for an order-up-to, two-stage supply chain process using simulation. They find that information sharing can reduce the bullwhip effect. Using agent-based modeling, Zarandi et al. (2008), derive ordering polices which can decrease the bullwhip effect. Dejonckheere et al. (2003) and Disney and Towill (2003) examine how to reduce the bullwhip effect using various fractional ordering policies. Gaalman (2006) extends this to the ARMA demand case when the lead-time is one period.

We note that in none of these cases does seasonal demand play a part in the analyses, which (a) has been shown to exist (Baltas, 2005; Beaulieu et al., 1992; Board, 2008; Heien, 1983; Wray, 1958) and (b) is incorporated in other supply chain research (Aviv and Federgruen, 2001; Bradley and Arntzen, 1999; Crowston et al., 1973; McClain and Thomas, 1977; Sethi and Cheng, 1997). We demonstrate in this paper that the relationship between lead-time and seasonal lags are crucial to bullwhip effect reduction.

From (19), we can derive, after some algebra, the following properties: (i) in the trivial case, if Φ = 0, then B(s, L, Φ) = 1, (ii) if s > L, then lim
                        l → 0
                     B(s, L, Φ) = 1, (iii) if s > L, lim
                        l → ∞
                     B(s, L, Φ) = 1 + (1 + 2Φ)/(1 − Φ), (iv) if s < L and Φ > 0, then B(s, L, Φ) > 1, and (v) if s < L and Φ < 0, then B(s, L, Φ) < 1.


                     Fig. 2
                      helps visualize these properties better. In Fig. 2a, the bullwhip function for varying Φ is plotted and each curve represents a different seasonal lag (s = 1, 2, or 4) when L = 2. Property (i) is clearly observed. Property (ii) is exhibited when s > L (s = 4, L = 2) and the dotted line is indistinguishable from a horizontal line at 1. Properties (iv) and (v) are visible for the s = 1 and s = 2 cases.


                     Fig. 2 b is a contour plot for the bullwhip measure showing changes in B(s, L, Φ) with differing values of L and s when Φ = 0.8. (Note that these contour plots should be interpreted at integer values only because s and L must be integers themselves.) We can see evidence of property (iii) here: as l = ⌊L/s⌋ increases, that is as we reach the top left corner of the figure, the bullwhip effect increases. Ultimately, the bullwhip effect exists (and non-negligibly so) if the seasonal lag s is much smaller than the lead-time L, and the AR parameter Φ is positive. Therefore, decrease L or set L < s to significantly reduce the bullwhip effect.

We skip the s = 1 case as it is simply the ARMA(1,1) setting, which has been analyzed before (Duc et al., 2008). Rather, we focus on the s > L ≥ 1 and L ≥ s > 1 cases. From (21) and (22) we can derive two properties: (i) if s > L ≥ 1, the bullwhip measure is not dependent on either lead-time or seasonality and exists only if θ > 0. Observe that B(s, L, Φ, θ) = 1 for all combinations of θ and Φ where θ(1 − Φ) = 0. This occurs if θ = 0 or |Φ| = 1, neither of which are possible given the restrictions on our parameters. Moreover, note that when θ < 0, B(s, L, Φ, θ) < 1. The second property (ii) occurs when L > s and in general, the bullwhip effect increases as l = ⌊L/s⌋ increases when Φ, θ > 0. In the limit, lim
                        l → ∞
                     B(s, L, Φ, θ) = [(1 + Φ)(1 + θ)2]/[(1 − Φ)(1 + θ
                     2)]. Furthermore, after some algebra, we can show that the bullwhip effect exists provided that θ > 0, regardless of the value of Φ. Note that the term m in (22) becomes irrelevant for large l.

                      illustrates these properties. The top two plots are contour plots of the bullwhip measure for varying values of θ and Φ. In Fig. 3a, the seasonal lag is shorter than the lead-time and we see the bullwhip measure increasing as θ and Φ increase (i.e., as we reach the top right corner of the graph), depicting property (ii). The seasonal lag is longer than the lead-time in Fig. 3b and we see that the bullwhip effect exists as long as θ > 0, showing property (i).

@&#CONCLUSION@&#

There is considerable evidence demonstrating the existence of seasonality in demand and has been incorporated into supply chain management analyses. We bring this vital issue to the bullwhip effect context by developing a theoretical measure for SARMA processes. We can derive an expression for such models by exploiting the ability to convert any SARMA model to the MA time series representation. Consequently, we can study the impact on the bullwhip effect when seasonal components to the demand process are introduced, resulting in a more realistic approach to the measurement problem. One inevitable disadvantage to this method is that the data requirements are higher than for ARMA models alone.

We find that the relationship between lead-time and seasonal lag to be the key to studying the bullwhip effect magnitude for SARMA models. In particular, provided that demand has been modeled, reducing the lead-time to below the seasonal lag can often reduce the bullwhip effect substantially. If altering the lead-time is not feasible, then a fractional ordering policy, such as the one proposed in Gaalman (2006), adjusted for the SARMA and L ≥ 1 settings, would be more appropriate.

To broaden our approach for chains with more than two stages, one could apply the ideas from Zhang (2004a) who shows that ARMA demand models result in ARMA order quantities, which is simply the demand for the supplier one step up in the supply chain. Finally, one could extend our theory by introducing demand shocks, as in Wang, Jia, and Takahashi (2005), and adding a model for variable lead-times as suggested by Chatfield et al. (2004). Both of these steps would make the theory better reflect what already occurs in practice.
