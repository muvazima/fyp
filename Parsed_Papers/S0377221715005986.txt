@&#MAIN-TITLE@&#Exploring the benchmarks of the Taiwanese investment trust corporations: Management and investment efficiency perspectives

@&#HIGHLIGHTS@&#


               
               
                  
                     
                        
                           
                           We utilize an additive efficiency decomposition (AED) data envelopment analysis (DEA).


                        
                        
                           
                           We assess the management and investment efficiencies of ITCs in Taiwan over the sample period 2007–2011.


                        
                        
                           
                           We use the AED DEA and network-based approach to distinguish and rank ITCs that can be treated as benchmarks.

@&#ABSTRACT@&#


               
               
                  Through an additive efficiency decomposition (AED) approach in data envelopment analysis (DEA), we evaluate the management and investment efficiencies of Investment Trust Corporations (ITCs) in Taiwan for the period 2007–2011. Furthermore, the AED-DEA and a network-based ranking approach in DEA are jointly used to rank and identify ITCs that can be treated as benchmarks. Frontier projections for ITCs are also highlighted. In general, our results demonstrate that the sample ITCs have higher investment efficiency than the management efficiency. Foreign ITCs appear to be the most efficient ones as compared to local ITCs and financial-holding ITCs. Finally, we construct a competitive map to help mutual fund managers improve their operating performance, resource allocation, and investment strategy formulation.

Since the Taiwanese mutual fund industry

                        In Taiwan, mutual funds, also known as an investment trust fund, are contract-type funds whose source of income is commission charges of fund management. Business of the Taiwanese investment trust industry mainly involves mutual funds, discretionary investments, and private equity funds, among which mutual funds accounts for the largest part.
                      was opened up to foreign investors in 1983, the number of investment trust corporations (ITCs) in Taiwan had increased from 4 to 38 firms in 2011. The 2011 Investment Company Fact Book released by the Investment Company Institute in 2012 also points out that the size of mutual funds issued in Taiwan increased from NT$54 billion in 1990 to over NT$1.8 trillion at the end of 2012. Specifically, not only the mutual fund industry in the U.S. has grown rapidly (Khorana, Servaes, & Tufano, 2005; Premachandra, Zhu, Watson, & Galagedera, 2012), but also that in Taiwan has exploded over the past few decades. Therefore, it is not surprising that the performance of ITCs has received much attention from researchers.

In the literature, significant inquiries into the fundamental question of the performance of ITCs, either at the individual level or the institutional level, can be observed; whereby mutual fund performance (Anderson, Brockman, Giannikos, & McLeod, 2004; Hsu & Lin, 2007; Shu, Yeh, & Yamada, 2002) and its determinants (Ferreira, Keswani, Miguel, & Ramos, 2013) are studied. Recent research has also examined the efficiency of mutual fund families in the United States (Premachandra et al., 2012). However, research on the performance of ITCs at the institutional level within Taiwan, another booming market, is particularly underdeveloped due to data unavailability. It is also worth noting that Khorana et al. (2005) claim that the securities trading environment of a country plays a role in determining the attractiveness of the ultimate investment vehicle. In other words, examining individually the performance of ITCs in Taiwan might provide us with insights regarding the performance of financial institutions.

Furthermore, the mutual fund industry in Taiwan allows us to determine the performance of different groups of ITCs. Harrigan (1985) contends that strategic group analysis enables a researcher to evaluate how firms obtain long-term profits. Lawless, Bergh, and Wilsted (1989) also find that different strategic groups have differences in competitive strategies and behavioral characteristics, and the competitive situation among groups changes over times (Fiegenbaum & Thomas, 1990). Therefore, the study divides ITCs into three strategic groups based on their operating characteristics for the purpose of discussing the effects of rivalry on performance in the mutual fund industry.

To measure the relative performance of ITCs in Taiwan, this study uses a two-stage data envelopment analysis (DEA) model. Traditional DEA models that are based on one-stage activities neglect intermediate measures or linking activities in a company and thus fail to render the message of economic activity management (Chen & Zhu, 2004; Färe & Whittaker, 1995; Tone & Tsutsui, 2009). Hence, many scholars have measured the performance of financial institutions through a two-stage production process (Kao & Hwang, 2008; Liu & Lu, 2010; Lo & Lu, 2006, 2009; Premachandra et al., 2012; Seiford & Zhu, 1999; Yang & Liu, 2012). This study utilizes the additive efficiency decomposition (AED) in the two-stage DEA model (Cook, Zhu, Bi, & Yang, 2010), which allows us to objectively measure the overall performance of ITCs as well as their performance in two individual stages. Consistent with Premachandra et al. (2012), the two-stage process of this study includes exogenous inputs in the second stage. Overall, we open up the ‘black-box’ process of performance evaluation by evaluating the management and investment efficiencies of ITCs in Taiwan.

In general, our findings suggest that ITCs in Taiwan have to first improve their management efficiency, and then they should focus on improving their investment efficiency. In addition, this study constructs a managerial decision-making matrix. This study is significant in the effect its insights may have on investment planning policy at the corporate level, in that it provides managers with suggestions to improve their operating efficiencies through a competitive map. By referring to the competitive map, ITCs can gain some inputs in allocating their resources. Moreover, the map can be applied as an analytical tool for operational improvement, investment-strategy planning, and overall business management. Furthermore, based on the benchmarking analysis in this study, the ITCs could have a good idea of their strengths and weaknesses to cultivate core competitive advantage and to produce the best possible results. That is, inefficient ITCs can learn from a benchmark ITC to work out winning strategies. The research results also could serve as references for investors and government fund supervision organizations at the governmental level in choosing excellent ITCs for entrusted management in the future.

An important consideration of concern in the DEA analysis is to increase the discrimination in the models (Liu, Lu, Yang, & Chuang, 2009). To rank and identify the true role models in the industry, we apply a network-based ranking approach in DEA (Liu et al., 2009) that is able to further distinguish the benchmark performance leaders. One of the key elements in the approach is the aggregation of DEA results for different input/output combinations so that the merits of each DMU under various situations can be considered. In other words, it can rank the efficient corporations in both stages and pinpoint the strengths and weaknesses of each organization (Liu & Lu, 2010). This study is novel in that we integrate the AED approach in the two-stage DEA model and ranking with the network-based ranking approach; we adopt the α centrality concept developed in the social network to point out the strong and weak inputs/outputs for each ITC; and we identify the top-rank benchmark ITCs for each input/output parameter.

The research results are informative to managers in the mutual fund industry on at least three counts. First, this study is significant in the effect its insights may have on investment planning policy at the corporate level, in that it provides managers with suggestions to improve their operating efficiencies through a competitive map. By referring to the competitive map, ITCs can gain some inputs in allocating their resources. Moreover, the map can be applied as an analytical tool for operational improvement, investment-strategy planning, and overall business management. Second, based on the benchmarking analysis in this study, the ITCs could have a good idea of their strengths and weaknesses to cultivate core competitive advantage and to produce the best possible results. The ranking results also could serve as references for investors and government fund supervision organizations at the governmental level in choosing excellent ITCs for entrusted management in the future. Third, managers are able to improve and adjust the allocation of internal organization's resources with an aim of realizing sustainable management. That is, inefficient ITCs can learn from a benchmark ITC to work out winning strategies.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 discusses related prior studies Section 3 introduces research design, including the sample selection criteria. Section 4 documents the empirical results and interpretations. Finally, Section 5 concludes this study.

@&#LITERATURE REVIEW@&#

In the literature, the concept of efficiency in financial institutions has been extensively discussed. Among frontier analysis method, DEA is one of the commonly used techniques (Berger & Humphrey, 1997; Liu, Lu, Lu, & Lin, 2013a, 2013b). To measure efficiency, most previous studies place emphasis on the production approach (Ferrier & Lovell, 1990; Sherman & Gold, 1985) or the intermediation approach (Haslem, Scheraga, & Bedingfield, 1999; Miller & Noulas, 1996). Previous studies that use DEA to investigate the relative efficiency of financial corporations are described as follows.


                        Sherman and Gold (1985) are among the first to apply the classical CCR model (Charnes, Cooper, & Rhodes, 1978), to evaluate the operating efficiencies of 14 branch offices of a savings bank. Their study has since invited a series of subsequent DEA studies on financial institutions. Rangan, Grabowski, Aly, and Pasurka (1988) introduce the two-stage contextual DEA method to measure the performance of banks. They conduct regression analysis after evaluating the efficiency of banks to explore the association between efficiency and bank characteristics.

The following three studies attempt to analyse efficiency changes over time. Elyasiani and Mehdian (1990) evaluate the efficiency of 191 large U.S. banks and compare their production frontier between 1980 and 1985. Berg, Førsund, and Jansen (1992) and Berg, Førsund, Hjalmarsson, and Suominen (1993) apply the Malmquist index to explore banks’ productivity growth in Norway and Nordic countries. Favero and Papi (1995) measure 174 Italian banks’ performance using the two-stage contextual method and show that efficiency is best explained by productive specialization, size, and location. Thompson, Brinkmann, Dharmapala, Gonzalez-Lima, and Thrall (1997) introduce several assurance region (AR) concepts to the related banking study.


                        Kantor and Maital (1999) integrate DEA with activity-based accounting to measure the costs and performance of the 250 branches of a large Mideast bank. Soteriou and Zenios (1999) use three models in evaluating the efficiency of bank branches, namely operation, service quality, and profitability. Paradi and Schaffnit (2004) adopt the production and strategic models to evaluate the performance of a Canadian bank. Giokas (2008) evaluates the performance of the branches of a Greek bank in three dimensions: production, transaction, and intermediation.

Several studies (Liu & Lu, 2010; Lo & Lu, 2006, 2009; Luo, 2003; Premachandra et al., 2012; Seiford & Zhu, 1999; Wanke & Barros, 2014; Yang & Liu, 2012) also apply the two-stage process concept to evaluate the performance of financial corporations. In the studies of Luo (2003), Seiford and Zhu (1999), and Lo and Lu (2006), banking operation is separated into the profitability and marketability stages. Recently, there has been a spate of interest in the study of performance evaluation of financial institutions using an AED approach in the two-stage DEA model, applying Chen, Cook, Li, and Zhu (2009) and Cook et al. (2010). For example, Liu (2011) examine the performance of Taiwanese financial holding companies, while Premachandra et al. (2012) document the efficiency of U.S. mutual fund families. Wang, Huang, Wu, and Liu (2014) study the efficiency measures of the Chinese commercial banking system.
                     

Overall, these studies reflect the methodological development of DEA. Although various studies have explored the two-stage performance of the finance industry, there is little convincing evidence on the performance of ITCs in Taiwan. Furthermore, the issues of ITCs’ performance topic and efficiency ranking are rarely discussed in the banking, finance and economics literature. That is, prior studies have never simultaneously (1) applied the AED approach in the network DEA method to explore ITCs’ overall performance; (2) combined the AED approach in DEA and the network-based ranking approach to rank efficient ITCs that can be treated as benchmarks; or even (3) investigated the influence of growth and competitiveness on the performance ITCs. Therefore, this study aims to provide some information regarding ITCs’ performance and ranking, with insights into resource allocation and competitive advantage, as well as ideas on strategic decision-making.

The business process of an ITC can be regarded as back-to-back operations of general management and investment management, and the benefits generated by the general management performance will exert its impacts on the performance of investment management. From the managerial perspective, it is therefore reasonable to analyze the efficiency of ITCs through two sub-processes and take the connectivity between the two sub-processes into consideration. A two-stage DEA model can explicitly measure the connectivity of ITCs’ internal economic activities and avoid the issue that the productivity in internal activities is ignored. Furthermore, operating performance should be evaluated based on the multi-index measurement to avoid inappropriate management decision (Bagozzi & Phillips, 1982; Chakravarthy, 1986). Therefore, based on this concept of multiple inputs and outputs as well as the two-stage idea used in prior studies (Chen & Zhu, 2004; Chen et al., 2009; Cook et al., 2010; Kao & Hwang, 2008; Liu, 2011; Lo & Lu, 2006, 2009; Luo, 2003; Premachandra et al., 2012; Seiford & Zhu, 1999; Sexton & Lewis, 2003; Wang et al., 2014; Zhu, 2000), this study proposes a two-stage production performance model for ITCs.

That is, the performance evaluation process of ITCs is divided into two stages, whereby the first stage measures the management performance of ITCs (hereafter referred to as management efficiency), while the second stage measures fund investment performance of ITCs (hereafter referred to as investment efficiency). The data were extracted from the Onshore Funds database, financial statements of the Securities Investment Trust and Consulting Association of the R. O. C. (SITCA) and Market Observation Post System (MOPS) of the Taiwan Stock Exchange, annual reports, and proxy statements published for the period 2007–2011. The study first identified 38 ITCs listed on the investment trust members’ directory. Companies with incomplete information and did not survive in the sample period were eliminated. As a result, the final sample contains 36 ITCs, including 11 financial holding ITCs, 17 foreign-funded ITCs, and 8 local-funded ITCs. Each ITC is treated as a DMU in the DEA analysis.

Descriptive statistics for the 36 ITC samples are provided in Table 1. To conserve space, we do not table the correlation coefficients for inputs and outputs in the two stages. First, the untabulated results reveal a significantly positive relation between inputs and outputs. The dataset satisfies the assumption of isotonicity; that is, an increase in any input should not result in a decrease in any output. Second, the DMUs were assumed to be “similar” and “calculable” units. In other words, when DMUs are homogeneous, the calculated results will be meaningful. Note that we use the two-stage DEA model to calculate the efficiencies of all the 36 ITCs in one sample and not in three samples (financial holding ITCs, foreign ITCs, and local ITCs) as they all operate under the same technology. Finally, Golany and Roll (1989) suggests that the numbers of DMUs are at least twice the total number of input and output variables. The sample DMUs in this study meet the requirements, i.e., 36 > 2 × (2 + 5 + 3) = 20; even if we follow another rule of thumb: the number of DMUs should be at least thrice the total number of input and output variables (Cooper, Li, Tone, Thrall, & Zhu, 2001), our sample DMUs also meet the requirements. In summary, the DEA model of this study is deemed valid.

In this study, we present the two-stage case for the model, in both the multiplier model and dual model forms. To generate the needed peer referencing information, the network-based ranking method requires the dual model form.

@&#RESULTS AND ANALYSIS@&#

Based on the controllable aspect of a manager, we employ the input-oriented AED approach in DEA (Chen et al., 2009; Cook et al., 2010) to evaluate the two-stage production processes of ITCs in a single implementation. One opting for DEA analysis should choose either the VRS or CRS model. As Avkiran (2001) suggested, the way of choosing either CRS or VRS is to run the performance models under each assumption and compare the efficiency scores. In this study, we apply the Wilcoxon matched-pairs test to perform the evaluation. The untabulated mean of the paired differences between CRS and VRS scores are not significantly greater than zero, thereby supporting the CRS assumption in the efficiency assessment.


                        Table 2
                         presents the results of management efficiency, investment efficiency, and overall technical efficiency (OTE). The score of relative efficiency ranges from 0 to 1. An ITC with a score of one is relatively efficient; one with a score of less than 1 is relatively inefficient. Table 2 shows that the average scores of management efficiency and investment efficiency over the five-year period are 0.463 and 0.937, respectively. These results suggest that policy makers in these ITCs should focus first on enhancing their utilization of management fees plus marketing and distribution fees, and then proceed to improve their investment strategies. That is, the findings indicate that the ITCs in Taiwan already operate at respectable levels in the stage of investment efficiency. The OTE shows a value of 0.928, meaning that ITCs in Taiwan has room for improvement of approximately 7.2 percent. Another finding worthy of note is that the trend of efficiency scores over the sample period. Generally, the efficiency scores fluctuate over the period with a sharp fall in 2008.

In Taiwan, ITCs are mainly divided into three groups in accordance with the background of group management structure, namely financial-holding ITCs, foreign ITCs, and local ITCs. As each category of ITCs has its unique features, it is another issue worthy of further exploration. To determine whether differences exist among financial-holding ITCs, foreign ITCs, and local ITCs in Taiwan on management efficiency and investment efficiency, we perform both parametric ANOVA F-test and non-parametric Kruskal–Wallis test. Table 3
                        
                         shows that the local ITCs have the lowest management efficiency, while the foreign ITCs achieve the best management efficiency among them. In terms of investment efficiency, the leading ITCs are still the foreign ITCs, followed next by local ITCs. On the overall basis, foreign ITCs have the highest efficiency of 0.954 as compared to that of local ITCs of 0.945 and that of financial-holding ITCs of 0.905. The differences are significant at the 10 percent significance level.

In summary, financial-holding ITCs are characterized by business diversification, large-scale organization, and centralized ownership. These ITCs are capable of integrating coordinated resources and enjoying diversified marketing channels, which facilitate their localization. Meanwhile, foreign ITCs possess an abundant supply of capitals and vast investment experience. They enter into the Taiwanese mutual fund industry through merger and acquisition or setting up of joint ventures with Taiwanese companies. With powerful support from their parent company, foreign ITCs have the advantage of integrating global research resources. With regard to local ITCs, they have no difficulty in cultural communication in the local market and have accumulated plenty of experience in issuing and underwriting business as these began earlier than the other two types. They are also more familiar with the features and development of Taiwanese stock market. Moreover, they have established a time-honored reputation and flexible marketing strategies as well as advantages in understanding the national conditions, developing retail investors, and completing product lines.

Benchmarking implies the method applied by companies to find the best role-model company and learn from it with an aim to effectively improve their own operating performance, as well as to analyze the gap between them and strengthen competitive advantages and operating performance through continuous improvement. This study combines the AED approach in DEA with the network-based ranking approach to perform benchmarking analysis by adopting the α centrality concept developed in the social-network analysis field. The benchmarks in different stages can be screened out, and the benchmarks of input items, intermediate items and output items can be further explored by multi-index evaluation.

Meanwhile, Capital, a local ITC, tops the efficiency in terms of turnover and 3-year standard deviation of returns. These results provide very practical information for ITCs. For example, with regard to the input items from Blackrock or FIL, the factors to benchmark include efficient utilization of management fees and marketing and distribution fees, respectively. The above discussion illuminates one merit of the network-based ranking approach — it tends to screen out specialized efficient corporations. In other words, it favors corporations that have their strengths evenly spread. Comparing among FIL, Capital, and Cathay in Table 5, one can recognize that Cathay has efficiencies in two intermediate factors and three output items. This makes Capital a winner over other ITCs although it has no any advantage in input factors. That is, the results show that although resources are conducive to improve corporate performance, it only in part generates effects if only one of the resources is applied.

Taken together, this study offers guidance for ITCs to prudently review the utilization of their internal resources, continuously maintain advantageous items, gradually upgrade weak areas by adjusting resource allocation, give full play to synergy effect with complementary resources, and substantially improve investment efficiency to promote competitive advantages and ultimately returns on investment.

Region A: ITCs with high competitiveness and fast growth include UBS and Ontario. These ITCs have scores of management efficiency and investment efficiency above 0.9. Currently, these ITCs are operating under excellent condition and they are improving rapidly. This signifies that they are on the right track. They should maintain both competitive advantage and sustainable growth to further improve internal weaknesses, if any.

Region B: Yuanta is the only ITC with high competitiveness and slower growth; its investment efficiency is between 0.6 and 0.9 and its management efficiency is above 0.9. Currently, it still enjoys a good managerial efficiency, even though growth is average. There is a need for Yuanta to learn from benchmark and try to pursue new breakthroughs to be able to improve its operating performance of investment and to maintain competitive advantage.

Region C: ITCs with medium competitiveness and fast growth include Franklin Templeton, FIL, Allianz, Blackrock, Mirae Asset, Schroder, Prudential, and Mega International. These ITCs have investment-efficiency scores greater than 0.9 and management-efficiency scores more than 0.6 but less than 0.9. Currently, these ITCs have a medium level of managerial efficiency. It means that the competitive advantage of these ITCs is gradually increasing. It may probably be because they are supported by enormous and abundant resources and they focus only on the overall prospect or policy of the group. However, problems like personnel integration, organizational difference, resource adjustment and allocation, and information system integration are not easy to be resolved in a short time. Hence, they need to continuously learn from benchmark groups and work out operation and management strategies, which will be embodied in investment performance in the long run.

Region D: ITCs with low competitiveness and fast growth include HSBC, Invesco, Pine Bridge, Reliance, Deutsche Far Eastern, JPMorgan, SinoPac, Cathay, Uni-President, Capital, Jih Sun, Fuh Hwa, Manulife, Paradigm, Taishin, and First Securities. These ITCs have investment-efficiency scores above 0.9 and management-efficiency scores less than 0.6. These ITCs are expected to have a bright prospect; however, they apparently lag behind other ITCs in terms of overall operating efficiency. Therefore, it is suggested that they should improve further their internal management efficiency. That is, priority should be given to internal operation and management to focus on long-term and stable operating strategies.

Region E: ITCs with low competitiveness and slow pace of progress include Fubon, Union, Hua Nan, Eastspring, KGI, Shin Kong, Truswell, Alliance Bernstein, and ING. These ITCs have investment-efficiency scores between 0.6 and 0.9 and management-efficiency less than 0.6. It is suggested that ITCs with low competitiveness and slow pace of progress should overcome the shortcomings by learning from benchmarks, prudently improving internal operating structures, reanalyzing market demands to map out investment guidelines and actively promoting fund operating performance. Slowly, they are able to catch up with other ITCs and strengthen competitive advantages of the company step by step.

The fund capacity, growth and operating performance of the investment trust industry are closely related to the development of Taiwan's investment trust industry and the liquidity and health of the stock and securities market. It can be found from the previous literatures that the DEA method has been widely applied in studies of performance evaluation of the financial industry, in particular, the analysis of the banking industry. But there are still several important issues yet to be explored. The securities investment trust industry is rarely taken as a research topic. From the perspective of research methods, traditional DEA models are one-stage models, and intermediate measures or link activities between the two stages are not given consideration. That is why the concept of a two-stage model is uncommonly discussed in the literature of ITC. Therefore, this paper adopts the AED approach in a network DEA, which is based on the Chen et al. (2009) model, to examine the management efficiency and the investment efficiency of ITCs in Taiwan. Furthermore, the network-based ranking approach successfully develops a scheme to rank the efficient ITCs in both stages and picks out the superior and inferior aspects of each ITC. An ITC will be able to know which area to improve on and from which organization to learn for a particular area.

Our main research results are summarized as follows: (1) The average scores of management efficiency and investment performance are 0.797 and 0.909. This suggests that the policy-makers should focus on improving corporate governance ability, enhancing administration activities and operational efficiency to improve their employees, operating expenses, and net fixed assets. (2) Ranks and strengths of the efficient unit at the two-stage. Union ranks first and second in terms of management efficiency and investment efficiency respectively, while UBS ranks third in management performance and fourth in investment performance. These two companies stand out in the rankings as they are influential and often used as reference by other ITCs. (3) A competitive decision-making matrix map is presented to assist ITCs to allocate resources in a better way and serve as an analytical tool for operational improvement, investment strategy planning and overall business management.

In the end, it is suggested that four government fund managers take ITCs in Region A as the first choice for fund outsourcing. It can be clearly seen from the research results that these ITCs are superior to others, and can be regarded as benchmarks, as they can produce the optimum benefits based on the current resources and help investors achieve investment objectives stably in the long run. It is also hoped that the methodology used in this study can bring about related research works in other industries.
